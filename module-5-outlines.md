## Voice & sourcing rules (locked from Modules 1–4, with Module-5 shift)

The voice locked in Modules 1–4 stays — conversational but precise, short paragraphs, invented concrete examples, aphoristic where natural. **Module 5 leans more directive.** Practical advice gets imperative sentences (*Set a timer. Write the count. Skip the scene that's stuck.*) — used sparingly and only where the directive is the lesson. Less "here's a framework," more "here's what you do, and here's why it works."

**Module-5 watch-points:**

1. **No productivity-bro register.** No *crush your goals*. No *here's the secret*. No listicle-energy openings. The writing process is humane work, not performative output. Where modern productivity content reaches for *hack*, *system*, *grind* — we use *practice*, *habit*, *the real cause*. Where it reaches for *transformation*, we describe specific weeks of typing.
2. **L1 vs L2 distinction.** L1 is about *starting*. L2 is about *sustaining*. Cross-lesson arc must keep the difference clean — the blank page is a single morning's problem, the first draft is a six-month problem.
3. **L4 checklist must explain itself.** Every item gets a one-sentence *why*. A checklist without explanation is a checklist a writer can't adapt to their own work; the lesson teaches the principle behind each item, not just the item.
4. **L5 needs teeth.** Concrete signs both ways. The diminishing-returns curve, the *next book* remedy, the specific things readers stop saying when a manuscript is done. Avoid soft-mode endings like *"trust your instincts."*

## Cross-lesson arc

Module 5 pivots the course from *what makes good fiction* (Modules 1–4) to *how do you actually do it* (Module 5). The arc is a complete production cycle:

| Lesson | Question | What it leaves the student with |
|---|---|---|
| 1. The blank page | How do I start *today*? | Three concrete tactics; a 25-minute exercise that produces 500 words |
| 2. Writing the first draft | How do I sustain over months? | A daily-wordcount habit, a separation between writer-mode and editor-mode |
| 3. Revision in 3 passes | How do I re-see the draft I finished? | Three sequential passes (structural → scene → line) and why the order matters |
| 4. Self-editing checklist | What do I check, specifically? | A checklist organised by pass, with a one-sentence why per item |
| 5. When to stop revising | How do I ship? | A diminishing-returns frame, three signs each way, and the *next book* remedy |

By Module 5's end the student has: a way to start a writing day, a habit that survives months, a re-seeing strategy that doesn't get stuck on commas before structure, an actionable checklist, and the discipline to let go.

The arc is start → sustain → re-see → check → stop. A complete production cycle, repeatable across drafts.

**Callbacks to Modules 1–4:**

- **L1 → M1 L3** (the blank page is sometimes information that the *premise* isn't ready — a writer can't draft a story whose engine they haven't found yet).
- **L2 → M2** (the structure work from Module 2 is here for *use*, not enforcement; a writer with a 3-act outline has a route through their first draft).
- **L3 → M3 L2 + M4 L4** (the load-bearing-detail test the student has now seen for backstory and for description is one of the line-level pass items).
- **L4 → M1 + M2 + M3 + M4** (the checklist is largely a list of *which earlier framework* you're checking against at each pass).
- **L5 → M1 L4** (a book is "done" when the three ingredients land — character cared about, conflict resolved or earned, change visible. The diminishing-returns frame closes the loop on the framework that opened the course).

**Within-module:**
- L2 references L1 once (the blank page returns at every chapter; the same tactics work).

- L4 sits *inside* L3 (the checklist is what you actually do during each pass).
- L5 returns to L3 (revision passes have a natural floor — when you stop catching things, you stop).

## Lesson 1: The blank page (overcoming it)

**Slug:** `process-blank-page` · **Length target:** 1,200–1,700 words

### Thesis
The blank page is a real problem, not laziness. Most writers solve it the wrong way — by waiting for inspiration, or by reading articles about the blank page instead of writing — and then call themselves blocked. The fix is mechanical: three tactics for getting words on the page in the next twenty-five minutes, plus the discipline to recognise when the blank page is actually telling you something useful (premise isn't ready, plan isn't there).

### Section structure

1. **The blank page is real** — *~200 words*
   - Sitting and not writing is not the same thing as choosing not to write. The fear is real. The perfectionism is real. The voice that says *anything you put down will be worse than nothing* is real.
   - This lesson is about working *through* it, mechanically. Not about pep talks.

2. **The real causes (not "writer's block")** — *~300 words*
   - The phrase *writer's block* hides the diagnosis. Most blank pages are one of four specific things:
     - **Fear of being bad.** The writer is protecting themselves from producing something embarrassing.
     - **Perfectionism.** The writer is waiting for the *right* sentence before starting; the right sentence does not arrive on a blank page, it arrives on the third bad sentence.
     - **Unclear premise.** The writer cannot draft a story whose engine they haven't found. Callback to [M1 L3](/learn/lesson/foundation-finding-idea) — when the blank page is *information* about the premise, the cure is back at premise level, not at typing level.
     - **No plan.** The writer doesn't know what *this scene* is supposed to do. They are trying to draft without knowing the function.
   - Each cause has a different fix. Naming the cause is half the cure.

3. **Three tactics that work** — *~350 words*
   - **Start in the middle of a scene.** Skip the throat-clearing. The first paragraph of any chapter usually gets cut anyway; write the second paragraph first, where the scene is already moving. *He set the cup down and said "I told you I would" before she finished asking the question.* You're already in.
   - **Write the easy scene first.** The scene you've been imagining for months — the one you can already see. It probably isn't chapter one; that's fine. Drafting in order is a convention, not a rule. The momentum from a scene you wrote well will carry you into the harder ones.
   - **Write a terrible first paragraph on purpose.** Aim for bad. Compete with yourself for the worst opening sentence you can produce. The fear of being bad cannot survive the requirement to *be bad*. Once the page is no longer blank, the second paragraph is a different problem.

4. **Constraints help** — *~250 words*
   - A blank page in front of unlimited time is harder than a blank page with a 25-minute timer.
   - **The timer.** Twenty-five minutes is short enough that the inner critic doesn't fully wake up. Longer sessions are useful; the critic learns to talk over them.
   - **The minimum word count.** Most writers do better with a small daily target they cannot fail (250 words) than a large one they sometimes hit (2,000). The target is for the habit; the habit is for the book.
   - **The skipped chapter.** If the chapter you're "supposed" to write next won't come, write the chapter after it. Mark the missing chapter with a placeholder. Come back when the surrounding pages have told you what the missing one needs to do.

5. **When the blank page is telling you something** — *~200 words*
   - Sometimes you are not blocked. Sometimes the premise isn't ready, or the scene doesn't have a function yet.
   - Sign: when you sit down to write the same scene three days in a row and produce nothing each time, the issue is rarely fear. The issue is usually that *the scene doesn't know what it's for*.
   - Cure: leave the page. Go back to outline level. Ask of the scene: *what does the protagonist want here, what's in the way, what changes by the end?* (Callback to M1 L4's three ingredients.) When you can answer those, the scene will write itself in twenty minutes.

6. **What this gives you** — *~150 words*
   - A diagnostic for the blank page (which of the four causes is this one?).
   - Three tactics that work for the first three causes.
   - The discipline to leave the page when the cause is the fourth.

### Try it

> **Exercise 5.1.1 — The 25-minute, 500-word session.** Set a timer for twenty-five minutes. Open a fresh document. Write 500 words on any scene from your story.
>
> Rules:
>
> - Do not stop typing.
> - Do not edit while writing.
> - Allowed: rambling, repetition, confusion, *[notes to self in brackets]*, restarting the same sentence three times.
> - The session does not produce *publishable* prose. It produces *prose*. That's what you need.
>
> If you produce 500 words, the page is no longer blank. If you produce 200 words, the page is no longer blank. Tomorrow's session is easier. The lesson is mechanical.

### Public-domain anchors

This is a craft lesson, not a literature lesson — there are no novels to anchor it to. Further reading is one resource on the writing life from the public domain.

- *An Autobiography* (Anthony Trollope, 1883). Brief, direct, and full of the most famous account of writing-as-job in the language. Trollope wrote two hundred and fifty words every quarter-hour before walking to his Post Office job. The book is short; read the chapter on his daily routine alone for the disenchantment of *blocked writer* as a category. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/5978](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/5978))

## Lesson 2: Writing the first draft (permission to be bad)

**Slug:** `process-first-draft` · **Length target:** 1,400–1,900 words

### Thesis
The first draft is for you, not for anyone else. The skill is keeping the *writer* (who generates) and the *editor* (who judges) from running at the same time. Bad first drafts are the standard, not the exception. Most novels die not from being badly written but from never being finished — the cure is the daily-wordcount habit, kept low enough that you cannot fail it.

### Section structure

1. **The first draft is for you** — *~250 words*
   - Nobody else will ever see it. Publishers see revised drafts. Agents see polished pages. Beta readers see something further along. The first draft has one reader, and that reader is the writer doing the next pass.
   - This is liberating. The first draft does not have to be good. It has to *exist*. Existence is the harder problem.

2. **The two voices** — *~300 words*
   - **Writer-mode** generates. It is willing to put down the wrong word. It is willing to write a scene that won't make the cut. It is willing to spend an afternoon on a chapter the writer will probably delete.
   - **Editor-mode** judges. It catches the wrong word. It refuses to keep going until the previous sentence is right. It is correct most of the time, which is the problem.
   - The two cannot run at once. Editor-mode running during writer-mode is the most common reason a first draft never finishes — every sentence is judged before the next one arrives, and the judging slows the writing past the rate at which a book gets written.
   - The fix is mechanical. While writing: do not edit the previous paragraph. Do not delete sentences. Do not re-read what you wrote yesterday before adding to it (or only briefly). Editor-mode comes back during revision, when it is welcome.

3. **The bad first draft is the standard** — *~250 words*
   - Almost every first draft is bad. Famous writers say this in interviews and almost no beginners believe it.
   - Believe it. The drafts you read on shelves have been through three to ten passes by the writer, then more by editors. The writer who put the first draft into print writes a different kind of book than the one you are trying to make.
   - This does not mean *try to be bad*. It means *don't expect to be good yet*. The good comes in revision. The first draft is the raw material the revision works on.

4. **Daily habit** — *~300 words*
   - The single most reliable upgrade to a writing life is a daily wordcount you cannot fail.
   - Pick a number low enough that you'd be embarrassed to miss it. Two hundred and fifty words is fine. Five hundred is fine. A thousand is on the high side; many writers can't sustain it, and an unsustained habit is worse than a sustained small one.
   - Anthony Trollope wrote two hundred and fifty words every quarter-hour before walking to his job at the Post Office, and produced forty-seven novels. The number was small. The consistency wasn't. *(Callback for those who want the full account: [Lesson 1's further reading](/learn/lesson/process-blank-page).)*
   - The habit is not about output. It is about *finishing*. Most novels die at month three because the writer skipped a week, then two weeks, then a month, and never came back. The habit makes the book inevitable.

5. **Outline vs discovery write** — *~250 words*
   - Some writers outline before drafting. Some discover the story by writing it. Both work.
   - Outlining suits writers who want a route map. The work in Module 2 — three acts, the hero's journey, beat sheets — is here for *use* in this draft, not for enforcement. An outline is a hypothesis about your story, not a contract.
   - Discovery writing suits writers who find their plot by drafting it. The trade-off: discovery drafts are often messier, longer, and require deeper structural revision. Plan accordingly.
   - The hybrid most professional writers use: a loose outline of major beats (inciting incident, lock-in, midpoint, dark moment, climax — the L1 of Module 2) and discovery writing the scenes between them.

6. **First-draft killers** — *~250 words*
   - **Editing as you go.** Slows the writing past the rate at which the book gets finished. Drafts die here.
   - **Showing drafts too early.** A first draft shown to a reader before completion almost always gets feedback the writer can't act on, and the feedback derails the draft. Wait.
   - **Comparing to published work.** The published novel is on its tenth pass. Your first draft is on its first. The comparison is dishonest; the writer punishing themselves with it stops writing.
   - **Skipping the daily wordcount.** Once is fine. Twice in a week is the start of the slide. Three times in a week and the book is at risk.

7. **What this gives you** — *~150 words*
   - The separation between writer-mode and editor-mode.
   - A daily habit small enough to keep.
   - The recognition that bad first drafts are standard, not failure.
   - Permission, when you need it, to write the worst chapter of your life and continue.

### Try it

> **Exercise 5.2.1 — Seven days of small.** Set a daily word count you'd be embarrassed to fail. Two hundred and fifty is fine. Three hundred is better. *Do not pick five hundred unless you know you'll hit it for seven days.*
>
> Write that count every day for seven days. The goal is not quality. The goal is the habit.
>
> At the end of the week, you will have between 1,750 and 2,100 words and a much clearer sense of what your daily writing looks like when it isn't optional. The writer who produces at this rate for a year produces a novel. The writer who skips three days a week never finishes one.

### Public-domain anchors

- *An Autobiography* (Anthony Trollope, 1883). The chapter on daily routine is the single most useful pre-1900 description of professional writing as a practice. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/5978](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/5978))

## Lesson 3: Revision in 3 passes

**Slug:** `process-revision-passes` · **Length target:** 1,400–1,900 words

### Thesis
You cannot fix a draft in one pass. Structural problems and line-level problems live at different scales, and trying to see both at once is why writers spend three months polishing prose in a chapter they will eventually cut. Three passes, in order — structural, scene, line — is the only reliable way through a revision.

### Section structure

1. **Why one pass doesn't work** — *~250 words*
   - You cannot simultaneously hold *does this chapter belong in the book* and *is this comma in the right place*. They are different mental modes. The structural mode is loose, abstract, willing to discard scenes; the line mode is tight, specific, in love with the sentence.
   - Run them at the same time and one wins. Usually the line mode wins, because the line mode produces visible progress (this paragraph is better!) while the structural mode produces invisible progress (this chapter is gone).
   - The cost of running them simultaneously is wasted polish. A perfectly worded paragraph in a chapter that will be cut is wasted work. Six months of wasted work is how a draft dies in revision.

2. **Pass 1: structural** — *~350 words*
   - One question: *does the story work?*
   - Read the manuscript fast. Take notes only on what *isn't working* at the chapter scale: scenes that don't belong, characters whose function is unclear, structural beats that are missing or weak.
   - Tools: a single sheet of paper. One sentence per scene, summarising what that scene *does* for the story. If you cannot write the sentence, the scene isn't doing work.
   - Output of Pass 1: a list of structural changes. Add a chapter. Cut a chapter. Move a chapter from after the midpoint to before it. Replace the antagonist's first appearance with a stronger one. *Don't fix anything yet.* Write the list.
   - Then make the changes. Add and cut. Move. Rewrite where needed *at the structural level only* — meaning rough scenes, not polished prose.
   - At the end of Pass 1 you have a draft whose architecture is sound. The prose may be terrible in places. That's expected. Pass 2 fixes those places.

3. **Pass 2: scene-level** — *~350 words*
   - The question for each scene: *does this scene earn its place?*
   - A scene earns its place if it has the three ingredients from [M1 L4](/learn/lesson/foundation-three-ingredients). Someone wants something. Something is in the way. Something has changed by the end.
   - Read the manuscript again, scene by scene. For each scene that does not have the three ingredients clearly, mark it. Either rewrite the scene to have them, or cut it.
   - Other Pass 2 checks:
     - Does the scene start late and end early? (Most beginner scenes start a paragraph too early and end a paragraph too late.)
     - Does at least one of the three checkpoints from M2 (inciting incident → lock-in → midpoint → dark moment → climax) live in this scene?
     - Are the right characters present? Could two characters be merged? (Callback to [M3 L4](/learn/lesson/characters-supporting-cast).)
   - Output of Pass 2: scenes that work as scenes, in a manuscript whose structure is already sound. The prose is *still* not polished. Pass 3 is where polish happens.

4. **Pass 3: line-level** — *~250 words*
   - Now, finally, the prose.
   - Read aloud. Read on paper. Read on a different device than the one you wrote on. The change of medium is the trick that lets you see what you've been re-reading without seeing.
   - Catch what the next lesson's checklist will catch — passive voice, repeated words, dialogue tag overuse, sentence rhythm, awkward grammar, any line that the ear flinches at.
   - Cut more than you think (callback to [M4 L4](/learn/lesson/world-description-pace) — *the painful part of writing is the cutting*).

5. **Time between passes** — *~200 words*
   - Let the draft cool. Two weeks is the floor; six is better.
   - You cannot revise a draft you can still recite. The mind reads what it expected to write, not what is actually on the page. Time off restores the ability to read what's there.
   - In the cooling period: write something else. Notes for the next book. A short story. Anything that re-engages writer-mode and lets editor-mode rest.

6. **Tools for re-seeing** — *~200 words*
   - **Print.** Reading on paper catches different problems than reading on screen.
   - **Different device.** A tablet, a phone, a friend's laptop — anything that breaks the visual pattern of the original document.
   - **Reading aloud.** The single most reliable way to catch awkward sentences. If your tongue trips on a sentence, the reader's brain trips on it too.
   - **Different room.** Sounds trivial; works.

7. **What this gives you** — *~150 words*
   - Three sequential passes — structural, scene, line — in that order, with cooling time between them.
   - The discipline to *not* fix sentences before you fix structure.
   - A specific output for each pass.

### Try it

> **Exercise 5.3.1 — Pass 1 only, on one chapter.** Take a chapter from your draft. On a single sheet of paper, write a one-sentence summary of what *each scene* in that chapter does for the story.
>
> *What does this scene do? — In one sentence.*
>
> If you can write the sentence in plain English, the scene is doing work.
>
> If you cannot, the scene is not yet earning its place. Mark it. Don't fix it yet — Pass 2 is where the fixes live. Pass 1 produces a list, not a rewrite.
>
> Doing this for one chapter is a fifteen-minute exercise. Doing it for a whole manuscript is the structural pass.

## Lesson 4: Self-editing checklist

**Slug:** `process-self-editing` · **Length target:** 1,300–1,800 words

### Thesis
A checklist that doesn't tell you *why* each item matters is a checklist a writer can't adapt. This lesson gives a working checklist organised by Pass (callback to L3) — every item with a one-sentence reason. The checklist is not exhaustive; it's load-bearing. Each item catches a problem the earlier modules taught you to recognise.

### Section structure

1. **A checklist organised by pass** — *~150 words*
   - Three sections, matching the three passes from L3: Structural, Scene, Line. Each item is a yes/no question. Each item has a one-sentence *why*.
   - The checklist is load-bearing — every item earns its place. There are not fifty items. There are about twenty.

2. **The structural pass checklist** — *~350 words*
   - **Is the protagonist's want clear by chapter 2 or page 30?** *Why:* readers form their investment from the want; without it on page one they don't yet care what happens. Callback to [M3 L1](/learn/lesson/characters-protagonist-wound).
   - **Is there a clear inciting incident in Act 1?** *Why:* the disturbance to the normal is what makes Act 2 necessary; without it, the story keeps introducing. Callback to [M2 L1](/learn/lesson/architecture-three-acts).
   - **Is there a lock-in by the end of Act 1?** *Why:* without a choice the protagonist cannot un-make, Act 2 has nothing to start from.
   - **Is there a midpoint reversal at roughly the middle?** *Why:* a sagging middle is almost always a missing midpoint.
   - **Is there a dark moment late in Act 2?** *Why:* the climax in Act 3 has nothing to climb out of without a floor in Act 2.
   - **Does the protagonist *change* by the end?** *Why:* the change ingredient is non-negotiable. A story whose situation changed but whose character didn't is a complaint, not a story. Callback to [M1 L4](/learn/lesson/foundation-three-ingredients).
   - **Does every act break feel earned?** *Why:* unearned act breaks make the reader feel manipulated.

3. **The scene-level checklist** — *~300 words*
   - **Does this scene have the three ingredients?** *Why:* a scene is a small story; without want, obstacle, and change it doesn't function as a unit of narrative.
   - **Does the scene start late and end early?** *Why:* most beginner scenes contain a paragraph of throat-clearing at each end that the reader skims.
   - **Are the right characters present?** *Why:* a scene with two characters who could be one is a scene that hasn't been edited yet (M3 L4).
   - **Is the conflict in the scene specific, not generic?** *Why:* "things go badly" is not a conflict; a specific want meeting a specific obstacle is.
   - **Does the scene either advance the plot or change the protagonist?** *Why:* if neither, the scene is filler — even if the prose is good.
   - **Is the setting doing work?** *Why:* a scene happens *somewhere*; the *where* should pull weight (callback to [M4 L3](/learn/lesson/world-setting-as-character)) or the scene's location is decoration.

4. **The line-level checklist** — *~400 words*
   - **Are dialogue tags mostly *said*?** *Why:* the eye trips on *exclaimed* and *muttered*; it does not trip on *said*. (M3 L5.)
   - **Are there fewer than three adverbs per page?** *Why:* most adverbs are stronger when cut. *She walked quickly* loses nothing as *she walked*; the sentence around it can do the rest.
   - **Are there fewer than two adjectives in a row?** *Why:* stacked adjectives signal a writer who didn't pick. *The cold, dark, empty room* should be one of those words; pick the one that does the most work and cut the others.
   - **Is the same word repeated within two paragraphs accidentally?** *Why:* the eye snags on accidental repetition; deliberate repetition is fine.
   - **Are sentences varied in length?** *Why:* paragraphs of all-similar-length sentences read as monotonous. Mix short, medium, long.
   - **Is description load-bearing?** *Why:* description that doesn't tell us about the character, set up something later, or carry needed atmosphere is cut. (M4 L4.)
   - **Does each scene engage at least two senses?** *Why:* the visual default flattens shown scenes (M4 L2).
   - **Have you read this aloud?** *Why:* the ear catches what the eye doesn't.

5. **The "out loud" test** — *~150 words*
   - Reading aloud is the single most reliable line-level diagnostic. The tongue trips on every awkward sentence. Sentences the eye accepts the ear refuses.
   - Most writers don't read aloud because it feels strange. Read aloud anyway. The strangeness is the lesson.

6. **When to stop checking** — *~150 words*
   - The checklist is for one revision pass through the draft, not for every paragraph forever. After one full line-level pass with the checklist, stop.
   - The next lesson — when to stop revising entirely — is the deeper version of this question.

### Try it

> **Exercise 5.4.1 — One chapter, line-level pass.** Take a chapter you've already revised. Run it through the line-level checklist above.
>
> Count how many fixes you find — places where dialogue tags are wrong, places where description doesn't earn its keep, places where adverbs could be cut.
>
> The number is information about your *blind spots*. Most writers consistently miss the same two or three categories. Knowing which two or three is what lets you self-edit faster on the next book.

## Lesson 5: When to stop revising

**Slug:** `process-when-to-stop` · **Length target:** 1,200–1,700 words

### Thesis
Books are not finished; they are released. A writer who cannot tell when to stop revising spends a year polishing one book while their next book waits unwritten. This lesson gives concrete signs both ways — three that say *done*, three that say *not done* — and the *next book* remedy for over-revising.

### Section structure

1. **The diminishing returns curve** — *~250 words*
   - The first revision pass on a draft is transformative. The second is large. The third is medium. The fourth is small. By the eighth or tenth, you are moving commas back and forth without adding meaning.
   - This isn't laziness; it's the curve. The work is real and useful for the first three to five passes. After that, a writer can spend forever and not produce a meaningfully better book.
   - The skill is to feel the curve. Past a certain point, each new pass produces less gain than the time would produce on a different book.

2. **Three signs you're done** — *~300 words*
   - **You're moving commas back and forth.** You changed a comma in chapter five last week. This week you changed it back. Next week you'll change it again. The book is past the point where your edits add meaning.
   - **You're rewriting passages you previously approved.** The novel is not getting better in those passages; you are seeing the same passage with different eyes on different days. This is normal and unending; the cure is to stop.
   - **Beta readers stop having structural notes.** Earlier readers said *this chapter is too slow* and *the antagonist isn't believable*. Recent readers are saying *I really liked the part where she chose*. When the structural feedback dries up, the structural work is done.

3. **Three signs you're not done** — *~300 words*
   - **You can't articulate what the book is about.** If you cannot say what the novel is *for* — its question, its argument, its central tension — in two sentences, the book hasn't found itself yet. Revision is needed.
   - **The ending feels rushed to *you*.** Beta readers may not catch this; you will. If you are rushing through the climax in your own re-reads because it doesn't feel earned, more work is needed there specifically.
   - **You're avoiding a specific section.** Most writers know which chapter they don't want to re-read. That chapter usually has a problem you haven't named yet. Read it. The problem will surface.

4. **Beta readers — calibrating their feedback** — *~200 words*
   - Three beta readers is usually enough; five is better; ten is too many. Each reader reads with their own preferences; aggregating across many becomes noise.
   - Trust the *patterns*, not the individual notes. If one reader says the antagonist is unbelievable, consider it. If three say so, fix it. If three say so but in different ways — one finds the antagonist too cruel, another too sympathetic, the third unclear — the antagonist isn't *unbelievable*; the antagonist is *unspecific*. The pattern is the diagnosis.

5. **Letting it go** — *~250 words*
   - At a certain point the manuscript stops being yours. You did the work; further work belongs to the editor, the publisher, the next book.
   - Books are not finished; they are released. The version you publish is the version you stopped working on; if you had kept going, the version a year later would be slightly different and probably not better.
   - The mindset that lets you ship is *this is good enough for the reader I imagined writing for*. Not perfect. Not the best version possible. Good enough that the reader the book is for will get what the book promises.

6. **The next-book remedy** — *~250 words*
   - The single most reliable cure for over-revising one book is starting the next one.
   - When the obsession has somewhere else to go, the previous book becomes shippable. The mind that was holding chapter twelve up to the light moves to drafting chapter one of something new.
   - Beginning writers often resist this — they feel the next book should wait until the first is "done." It won't be done in the way they're thinking. The next book is what makes the first one done.
   - This is the loop the rest of the course was building toward. You used the framework (M1) to find a premise. You built the architecture (M2). You wrote the characters (M3). You rendered the world (M4). You drafted, revised, and shipped (M5). Then you started over.

### Try it

> **Exercise 5.5.1 — Define "done" for your draft.** Write three specific, observable conditions that would tell you your current draft is shippable.
>
> *Specific* means a beta reader could verify it. *Observable* means it doesn't depend on your mood that day.
>
> Vague conditions don't count:
>
> - *"When it's good"* — too vague.
> - *"When I'm happy with it"* — depends on the day.
> - *"When the chapters all flow"* — what does *flow* mean operationally?
>
> Concrete conditions count:
>
> - *"When three of three beta readers identify the same emotional climax."*
> - *"When I read chapter five aloud without stopping to fix anything."*
> - *"When I cannot, on a fresh read, name a structural change I would make."*
>
> If you cannot write three concrete conditions, your sense of "done" is too vague to ever arrive at. The exercise is the start of having one.

## Quiz preview (5 questions, 70% to pass — applied register)

Drafted in the M1 widow-and-bees style. Each tests *understanding* via a scenario.

**Q1.** A writer sits at the same blank chapter for three days. Each session, they produce nothing. Per Lesson 1, what is most likely happening, and what's the diagnostic move?
- (a) The writer is blocked; they should take a long break.
- (b) The writer is probably *not* blocked in the fear/perfectionism sense — three days of producing nothing on the *same* scene usually means the scene doesn't have a clear function yet, and the cure is to leave the page and ask of the scene: what does the protagonist want here, what's in the way, what changes by the end? ✓
- (c) The writer should write through it with longer sessions.
- (d) The writer should give up the project.

*Explanation:* (a) and (d) overcorrect. (c) treats the symptom without the diagnosis. (b) is L1's specific diagnosis: the four causes are fear, perfectionism, unclear premise, and no plan. *Same scene, three days* almost always means the fourth — no plan. The cure is at outline level, not at typing level.

**Q2.** A writer's first draft has been going well for six weeks, then stalls. They notice they've started re-reading yesterday's pages before adding new ones, fixing sentences as they go. Per Lesson 2, what is most likely happening?
- (a) The draft is failing; they should start over.
- (b) Editor-mode has begun running during writer-mode, slowing the writing past the rate at which the book gets finished — the cure is to stop re-reading yesterday's pages and to forbid in-draft editing until the first draft is complete. ✓
- (c) The writer needs more inspiration.
- (d) The writer should outline more thoroughly.

*Explanation:* (a) misreads the symptom. (c) is vague. (d) is unrelated to the specific failure mode. (b) is L2's specific diagnosis: editing as you go is the most common reason a sustained first draft stalls. The cure is the separation of writer-mode and editor-mode — editor-mode comes back at revision.

**Q3.** A writer has finished a first draft and spends three months polishing the prose chapter by chapter. At the end of three months, they realise chapters 4-6 don't belong in the book and need to be cut. According to Lesson 3, what is the lesson?
- (a) Always start with a more detailed outline.
- (b) Revision should never include cuts at the structural level.
- (c) Pass 1 (structural) must come before Pass 3 (line-level); polishing prose in chapters that will be cut is wasted work, which is exactly the failure mode L3 prescribes the three-pass order to prevent. ✓
- (d) The writer should hire an editor sooner.

*Explanation:* (a) is irrelevant to the revision question. (b) is the opposite of the truth. (d) is a workaround. (c) is L3's specific lesson: the three passes are *structural → scene → line*, in that order. Polish before structure means the writer polishes scenes that won't survive the structural pass. Three months of wasted work is the canonical example.

**Q4.** A writer's checklist has thirty items, including "use vivid verbs," "be original," and "make the reader feel something." Per Lesson 4, what is wrong with this checklist?
- (a) Thirty items is too few; checklists should have at least fifty items.
- (b) The items are not actionable — they describe outcomes (*vivid*, *original*, *feel something*) rather than specific things the writer can check on the page; an actionable checklist asks yes/no questions tied to specific craft moves, with a one-sentence why per item. ✓
- (c) The items are too specific; checklists should be general guidance only.
- (d) Checklists are unnecessary; trust your instincts.

*Explanation:* (a) misreads the failure mode. (c) is the opposite of the lesson. (d) abandons the lesson entirely. (b) is L4's specific point: a checklist with vague outcome-items can't be applied. *Use vivid verbs* doesn't tell you what to look for; *Are dialogue tags mostly "said"?* does — and the *why* (the eye doesn't trip on said) lets the writer adapt the rule.

**Q5.** A writer is on revision pass nine. They moved a comma in chapter five last Tuesday; this Tuesday they moved it back. Three beta readers have read the latest draft and none of them have structural notes. Per Lesson 5, what is most likely true, and what's the move?
- (a) The book isn't ready; the writer should keep revising until they don't move the comma back and forth.
- (b) The book is past the point of useful revision — the comma being moved back and forth is one of the three signs of *done*, the absence of structural beta-reader notes is another, and the cure for over-revising one book is to start the next. ✓
- (c) The book needs more beta readers.
- (d) The book needs a developmental edit.

*Explanation:* (a) inverts the diagnostic. (c) treats noise as the cure to clarity. (d) is a workaround that doesn't address the question of *when to stop*. (b) is L5's specific diagnosis: *moving commas back and forth* and *no structural notes from readers* are two of the three signs of done. The remedy is the next-book remedy. The current book is shippable.
