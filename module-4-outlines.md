## Cross-lesson arc

Module 4 moves from *foundation* → *granularity* → *function* → *discipline*. The student begins with the show/tell choice that shapes every paragraph, learns to make the chosen moments multi-sensory, learns when setting earns its way into the role of character, and ends with the cull — how much of all this work belongs in the final draft.

| Lesson | Question | What it leaves the student with |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Show, don't tell — the real meaning | When do I show, and when do I tell? | A clear test: the moment that *matters* gets shown; the rest gets told |
| 2. Sensory writing | How do I make a shown moment land? | A two-sense floor for every important scene; a vocabulary for the four under-used senses |
| 3. Setting as character | When does the world become a character in its own right? | The conditions under which setting carries weight; one perfect detail vs. ten generic |
| 4. Description without slowing pace | How much of all this belongs in the final draft? | The load-bearing-detail test applied to every description; the 1-sentence room as the default |

By the end the student has: a sentence-level understanding of where their pen choices come from (show vs tell), a multi-sensory technique for the moments they choose to render, a way to elevate setting when the story can hold it, and the discipline to cut almost all of the rest.

**Callbacks to Modules 1–3:**

- **L1 → M1 L2** (the empathy machine — *showing* engages the reader's simulator more strongly than *telling*; that's why it feels more vivid, and also why it's more expensive in page count).
- **L2 → M3 L5** (voice differentiation rises from the wound; sensory choices rise from the same place — what a character notices is downstream of what they protect).
- **L3 → M3 L3** (system-as-antagonist: a setting that opposes the protagonist is just a system with a face the writer is willing to render).
- **L4 → M3 L2** (the load-bearing test: backstory earns its way onto the page by being causal; description earns its way by doing structural work — both fail the same way and the cure is the same cut).

**Within-module callbacks:**
- L2 references L1 (sensory detail is *show* working at line level; without it, "show" is a slogan).
- L3 references L1 + L2 (a setting that does work has been *shown* multi-sensorily; abstract claims about a place don't earn the elevation).
- L4 references all three (description is the synthesis: show enough, tell where you can, render senses, elevate setting where it earns it, cut the rest).

## Lesson 1: Show, don't tell — the real meaning

**Slug:** `world-show-dont-tell` · **Length target:** 1,300–1,800 words

### Thesis
*Show, don't tell* is the most quoted and most misapplied piece of writing advice in publishing. The real rule is: *show what matters; tell what doesn't*. Showing is slow and vivid. Telling is fast and informative. Both belong in every novel, doing different jobs. The skill is knowing which moment wants which tool.

### Section structure

1. **The misquote** — *~250 words*
   - The advice is usually delivered as if *telling* were a sin. It isn't. Telling is what gets you across a year of fictional time in a paragraph. *The next twelve months passed quickly; by spring she was no longer flinching when his name came up.* That sentence does in fifteen seconds what showing would need pages to do.
   - The advice survives because beginners over-tell — they summarise the moment that should be rendered. The cure isn't *show everything*. The cure is *show the moments that matter, and only those*.

2. **What showing actually is** — *~300 words*
   - Showing renders a moment in real time. The reader is given the actions, the dialogue, the sensory detail; they reach the meaning by inference.
   - It is *expensive*. A scene that could be told in two sentences might take two pages to show. That cost is the price of vividness — the reader's emotional investment is doubled when they reach the meaning themselves.
   - Inline mini-demonstration: *She set the cup down. Her hand was shaking. Outside, snow had begun to fall.* Three sentences; the reader infers that something has just happened that has shaken her. We haven't told them she is upset.
   - This is what M1 L2 called *the empathy machine* working at line level. Showing pulls the reader's simulator on; telling lets it idle.

3. **What telling actually is** — *~300 words*
   - Telling compresses or summarises. It moves time, it bridges scenes, it gives the reader the information they need without spending the page count of a rendered moment.
   - Telling is what makes a novel feel like *a novel* and not like a stage play. The novelist's privilege over the playwright is summary — the ability to say *the next year passed quickly* and to mean it.
   - Telling is also how interiority happens at speed. A character's history, a character's frame of mind between two scenes, the texture of a relationship that is not currently on stage — these are told. To show them every time would balloon the book.

4. **The real rule: show what matters, tell what doesn't** — *~300 words*
   - Three questions to ask of any scene that is currently being *told*:
     - Is this moment a turning point?
     - Will the reader's emotional investment depend on having lived through this on the page?
     - Is the reader currently being *told* something they should be *feeling*?
   - If yes to any: show it.
   - If no: keep telling. Move on.
   - Three questions to ask of any scene currently being *shown*:
     - Is this moment a turning point — or merely a transition?
     - Could the reader catch up in two sentences?
     - Is the showing rendering a moment, or is it a writer in love with their own prose?
   - If the moment is a transition or could be summarised, *tell* it. Save the showing for the moments that matter.

5. **The over-correction: showing everything** — *~250 words*
   - A draft in which every scene is shown is exhausting. The reader can't feel the difference between a turning point and a transition because everything is being rendered with equal weight. The reader's emotional pacing flattens. They put the book down.
   - The cure is the cut — or, more often, the rewrite of a scene from *show* to *tell*. The exercise at the end of this lesson is the practice version of this move.

6. **Public-domain illustrations** — *~200 words*
   - In *Pride and Prejudice*, the engagements are mostly *told*. *"It was settled between them at last that they should be married."* That sentence covers what other writers would render as a scene. Austen's choice is not laziness; it is *focus* — the engagements are not where her interest lies; the misjudgements that came before them are.
   - In *Wuthering Heights*, the moors are *shown*. The same novel that summarises whole years in a sentence will spend two pages on the wind across heather. The moors are where the novel's interest lies; everything else is told around them.

7. **What this gives you** — *~150 words*
   - A test for every paragraph in your draft.
   - Permission to *tell* — which is what most beginners need before they can correctly *show*.
   - The next three lessons of this module are downstream. Sensory writing (L2) is what makes the shown moments land. Setting (L3) is when the *world itself* becomes a moment that wants showing. Description pacing (L4) is the discipline of cutting back to what earned its place.

### Try it

> **Exercise 4.1.1 — Two versions of the same paragraph.** Take a paragraph from your work in progress that *shows* a moment. Rewrite it as a paragraph that *tells* the same content. Be honest about what you lose; be honest about what you gain in pace.
>
> Then ask: which version belongs in your novel? The answer is sometimes the original. It is sometimes the rewrite. It is occasionally — usefully — neither, and the rewrite has shown you what the third version would be.

### Public-domain anchors

- *Pride and Prejudice* (Austen, 1813) — masterclass in productive *telling*.
- *Wuthering Heights* (E. Brontë, 1847) — for the moors as the moments worth *showing*.
- *Madame Bovary* (Flaubert, 1856; the 1886 Eleanor Marx Aveling translation is PD) — for the famous textbook of when to render and when to summarise.

## Lesson 2: Sensory writing (the 5 senses technique)

**Slug:** `world-sensory-writing` · **Length target:** 1,300–1,800 words

### Thesis
Most writers default to visual. The four other senses are radically underused. A scene that engages two or more senses lands harder than a scene that engages one — even when the second sense is delivered in a single phrase. This lesson teaches the technique and demonstrates it in inline examples.

### Section structure

> **Note for Phase B drafting:** L2 must demonstrate sensory writing in inline examples, not just describe it. Plan: one 4–5-sense paragraph as the centerpiece demonstration, plus 1–2 short examples for specific senses (smell, sound). All inline examples will be invented.

1. **The visual default** — *~200 words*
   - Open a beginner's draft to a random scene. Count the senses engaged. Almost always: vision, vision, vision, the occasional sound.
   - Vision is the easiest sense to write because it's the easiest sense to remember from the writer's own life. The other four are harder; they require deliberate attention.
   - The reader, however, lives in five senses. A scene that engages only one is a scene that feels *flat* even when the prose is good. The reader doesn't know why; they just close the book sooner than they meant to.

2. **A multi-sensory demonstration** — *~250 words*
   - **Inline demonstration here in Phase B.** A short paragraph (maybe 50–60 words) that engages four or five senses in a single moment. Likely: a kitchen scene with a radiator ticking (sound), butter softening (sight + touch), strawberries on the windowsill (smell + implied taste), a footstep on the stairs (sound). The point is to *show* the technique working before *naming* it.
   - The principle: engaged senses don't have to dominate the paragraph. A single phrase per sense is enough. *A faint sweetness from the windowsill* is the entire smell — eight words — and it does as much work as a paragraph of visual detail because it's the only smell in the room.

3. **Smell — the fastest emotional trigger** — *~250 words*
   - Smell is the most direct route to memory. A reader who reads *the smell of damp wool and chalk dust* will be in a primary-school classroom faster than three paragraphs of visual description could place them there.
   - Smell is also under-written because writers can't remember smells the way they remember sights. The cure is to ask, of every important scene: *what does the room smell like, and what does that smell mean to the protagonist?*
   - Inline mini-example: *He didn't know which detail bothered him more — the cold tea or the faint cigarette smoke that shouldn't have been there.* The smoke is one phrase; it changes the meaning of everything else in the scene.

4. **Sound — for atmosphere** — *~250 words*
   - Sound builds atmosphere faster than sight. A scene with the wrong soundscape feels off even when nothing else is wrong.
   - The trick is to use sound as a *frame* — not as something the protagonist consciously notices, but as something the reader feels at the edges. *Outside, a car passed and the windows shook a little.* (Callback: this is the technique in M3 L5's subtext demonstration; the sound carried the emotional layer.)
   - Sound is also how a writer signals time of day, weather, and mood without describing them directly. A scene that opens *the kitchen was warm and the only sound was the kettle starting to sing* has placed itself before the writer has named where the reader is.

5. **Touch — for intimacy and physical presence** — *~250 words*
   - Touch is the sense that puts the reader *in the body*. A character whose hands the reader has felt — the cold of a railing, the slick of a wet handle, the give of fabric — is a character the reader inhabits.
   - Touch is also how a writer renders a body's wear without naming it. Stiff knees on stairs. The way a thumb finds a familiar callus. These are the sentences that age a character without an age in years.

6. **Taste — for memory** — *~150 words*
   - Taste is the rarest sense in fiction because most scenes don't centre on food. When taste shows up, it usually does memory work — a tea, a particular bread, the taste of a place.
   - Use sparingly. A scene with three tastes feels like a recipe. A scene with one taste — the right one — can do what a paragraph of summary couldn't.

7. **The two-sense rule** — *~200 words*
   - Every important scene needs at least two distinct senses engaged. *Important* means the scene is being *shown* (callback to L1), not told.
   - The rule is a floor, not a ceiling. Most scenes won't need five senses; many will work brilliantly with two well-chosen ones (sight + sound, sight + smell, sight + touch).
   - In revision, scan for scenes engaging only one sense. Add one sense — a single phrase — and watch the scene gain weight.

8. **What this gives you** — *~150 words*
   - A diagnostic for every important scene: *which two senses?*
   - A vocabulary for the under-used four.
   - A specific cure for the *flat* feeling that beginner drafts have.
   - The next lesson elevates the *world* from background to character — sensory writing is the technique that makes that elevation possible.

### Try it

> **Exercise 4.2.1 — Mark and supplement.** Take a scene you've written (one that's *shown*, not told — see L1). With a marker, highlight every sensory detail. Note the sense beside it: V, S (sound), T (touch), Sm (smell), Ta (taste).
>
> Identify which senses are missing. Pick the one that would matter most for *this* scene — *what's the smell of this room? what sound is at the edge?* — and add it. A single phrase.
>
> Compare the two versions. The added phrase usually doubles the scene's grip on the reader without adding to its length by more than five percent.

## Lesson 3: Setting as character

**Slug:** `world-setting-as-character` · **Length target:** 1,400–1,900 words

### Thesis
Sometimes a setting is more than backdrop. The moors in *Wuthering Heights* are not a stage on which the novel's events happen; they are an *agent*. They oppose the characters. They reflect the characters. They outlive the characters. When a setting carries that kind of weight, the rules for it change. This lesson teaches when to grant setting that elevation, what concrete tools to use to do it, and where the failure mode (pretentiousness) lives.

### Section structure

1. **What "setting as character" actually means** — *~250 words*
   - Setting becomes character when the place does *work* in the story that an inert backdrop couldn't. It opposes the protagonist. It withholds. It changes when the protagonist changes. It outlasts them.
   - This is not a vague claim about *the soul of the place*. It is a structural claim. The setting acts. The setting behaves. The setting can be characterised by the same wound/want/need framework you used for people in [M3 L1](/learn/lesson/characters-protagonist-wound) — though loosely; the point is that the setting has *temperament*, not psychology.

2. **Setting that opposes** — *~300 words*
   - The cleanest case. The setting is the antagonist, or part of it.
   - The moors in *Wuthering Heights* are indifferent, beautiful, and willing to kill you in a storm. The novel is about people who love the moors and are formed by them; it is also about people the moors are inflexible toward.
   - The London fog in *Bleak House* is the same. It is not a metaphor; it is a fact. It enters every chapter. It slows the law courts. It thickens around the characters at the moments their lives are thickening.
   - Callback to [M3 L3](/learn/lesson/characters-antagonist) (system as antagonist): a setting that opposes is a system with a face the writer is willing to render. The fog is *poverty* and *the law* and *the city* in concrete form, but rendered as fog — touchable, see-able, breath-able. The system carries the weight; the rendered fog makes it confrontable.

3. **Setting that mirrors** — *~300 words*
   - The setting changes alongside the protagonist. Pathetic fallacy — weather that matches mood — is the textbook version. Done badly it is cliché (rain at funerals). Done well it is one of the strongest tools in the writer's kit.
   - The trick is to make the mirror *unstable*. The weather agrees with the protagonist some of the time, contradicts them at the worst times, ignores them at others. A consistent mirror is wallpaper. An inconsistent mirror is a presence.
   - The marsh in *Great Expectations* is a mirror that does this work. It mirrors Pip's class anxiety, his fear of the convict, his eventual shame. It also fails to mirror — there are sunny days when Pip is miserable, and storms when he is briefly at peace. The marsh is not Pip; it is *with* Pip.

4. **Pathetic fallacy: when it works, when it's cliché** — *~250 words*
   - It works when the weather is *specific* and *unexpected*. Not "the storm raged as he walked away." Try: *The wind kept putting the rain into his face from a different angle every time he turned, as if the weather had its own opinions about which way he should go.*
   - It is cliché when the weather is *generic* and *predictable*. Rain at funerals. Sun at weddings. Mist before mysteries. The reader has seen the pairing too many times; the writer has saved themselves the work of choosing a specific weather.
   - The cure is specificity. Pick one weather detail no other writer has used for this kind of moment. Render it concretely. Trust the reader to feel the resonance.

5. **One perfect detail beats ten generic** — *~250 words*
   - The instinct, when elevating setting to character, is to lavish description. Resist it.
   - The setting that *carries weight* in fiction usually does so through a few perfect details, not through extensive description. The fog in *Bleak House* is not described for paragraphs every time it appears; it is named, given a single specific behaviour, and trusted to do its work.
   - One sentence about the way the moor looks after a frost can do more than a page of moor description. The right detail compresses; the wrong details accumulate without weight.

6. **When setting goes too far** — *~250 words*
   - There is a failure mode where the writer falls in love with the world and renders it past the story's needs. The world becomes too detailed for the story to breathe inside it. The reader feels they are reading a setting catalogue, not a novel.
   - The cure is the load-bearing test from [M3 L2](/learn/lesson/characters-backstory): does this detail explain a present-day choice, or set up a present-day moment, or carry an emotional weight that affects the protagonist? If yes, keep. If no, cut, even if it is beautiful.
   - The world is not the point of your novel. The protagonist's experience of the world is the point. A world that is bigger than the protagonist drowns them.

7. **Pretentiousness — and the cure** — *~150 words*
   - The failure mode of *setting as character* is the abstract claim. *The city was a being unto itself, alive and watching.* That kind of sentence flatters the writer and bores the reader.
   - The cure is concreteness. *On the third day in the city, he noticed that the bus drivers all knew each other's first names, and none of them used his.* That is the city as character — through one specific detail that does work the abstract claim couldn't.

8. **What this gives you** — *~150 words*
   - The conditions under which a setting earns elevation to character status (it acts, it withholds, it changes, it persists).
   - The two main modes (opposing, mirroring) and the rules for each.
   - The discipline that prevents the elevation from collapsing into purple prose: one perfect detail beats ten generic.

### Try it

> **Exercise 4.3.1 — Two views of the same place.** Identify your story's primary setting. Write 100 words about it from your protagonist's perspective at the *beginning* of the story, and 100 words from their perspective at the *end*. The setting itself hasn't changed; their relationship to it has.
>
> If the two passages read identically, the setting isn't doing character work yet. Revise: change the *details the protagonist notices* between the two passages. The same room, seen by the same person, after the journey of the novel, contains different things for them. That difference *is* the setting acting as character.

## Lesson 4: Description without slowing pace

**Slug:** `world-description-pace` · **Length target:** 1,200–1,600 words

### Thesis
Description is what kills first drafts. Most beginners write three to five sentences when one would do. The cure is the *load-bearing detail* test (already used in [M3 L2](/learn/lesson/characters-backstory) for backstory): every description earns its keep by doing structural or emotional work, or it goes. A novel paced for its reader is a novel that has accepted, painfully, that *the writer's favourite paragraph* is rarely the reader's.

### Section structure

1. **The problem** — *~200 words*
   - First drafts almost universally suffer from over-description. The room is described when only one detail mattered. The character's outfit is described when only the necktie was load-bearing. The weather is described twice because the writer forgot they'd already done it.
   - The reader feels these passages as *slowing* — the page count between events grows, attention drains, the book gets put down.
   - The cure is not "less description." The cure is *load-bearing description*. Every detail earns its place by doing work; everything else is cut.

2. **The load-bearing-detail test** — *~250 words*
   - Same test as the M3 L2 backstory test, applied to description. Take any passage of description in your draft. Ask:
     - Does this detail tell the reader something about the *character* (their values, their attention, their state)?
     - Does this detail set up something *later* in the scene or the chapter?
     - Does this detail carry *atmosphere* that the scene needs and isn't getting from elsewhere?
   - Three yeses: keep all of it.
   - One yes: cut to a single sentence.
   - Zero yeses: cut entirely.
   - This sounds extreme. It is. The vast majority of description in a first draft does no work. Cutting it is the difference between a 120,000-word draft that can't find an agent and a 90,000-word novel that gets read.

3. **The 1-sentence room** — *~250 words*
   - The default for any room a character enters: one sentence.
   - *He came in through the back door of a kitchen that hadn't been changed in thirty years.* Eighteen words. The reader has the room. Move on.
   - Most rooms in a novel are 1-sentence rooms. The character is there for a beat; the room services the beat; the next thing happens.
   - The exceptions — kitchens that get five sentences, ballrooms that get a paragraph — are the *load-bearing rooms*. Use the elevation only when the room is doing more than holding the character.

4. **Embedding description in action** — *~250 words*
   - The strongest description is invisible. The character moves through the room, and the room arrives by *what they touch and notice*.
   - *She set the cup down on the counter. The radiator was warm against her elbow. She pressed her hand to it, briefly, and went to find her coat.* The kitchen has been described — counter, radiator, warmth — without a single sentence of standalone description.
   - Embedded description carries character. *What* the character notices is who they are. The character who notices the radiator is different from the character who would notice the magnet on the fridge. The character who notices the chip in the counter is different again. Description is an opportunity to render character at the same time.

5. **When to lavish (key locations, transformative moments)** — *~200 words*
   - Some places earn extended description: the protagonist's home (beginning of novel), the place of the wound (when it surfaces, often at the midpoint), the location of the climax. These are load-bearing rooms that get elevated description because the elevation *is* part of how the reader feels the moment's weight.
   - Even here: edit. Five sentences is usually too many. Three carefully chosen sentences usually outperform five competent ones.

6. **Cutting in revision — almost always more than you think** — *~250 words*
   - A useful exercise on a stuck chapter: cut every adjective. Read the chapter back. Almost all of the cuts will read better than the original. The few that don't, restore.
   - The same for adverbs. The same for sentences whose only function is description. A novel that has been through this pass once is a novel whose pace has just gone up by twenty percent.
   - Most beginners fight this exercise because the cuts feel like loss. They aren't. They are *concentration* — the same word the L2 cut exercise used in M3.

7. **What this gives you** — *~150 words*
   - The 1-sentence room as the default.
   - A test for every passage of description: load-bearing or cut.
   - The skill of embedding description in action so it carries character at the same time.
   - The discipline to *cut more than you think* in revision — the single most reliable upgrade available to a first draft.
   - This is the last lesson of Module 4. You now have, for your draft: the show/tell choice (L1), the multi-sensory technique (L2), the elevation of setting when the story can hold it (L3), and the discipline to keep all of it lean (L4).

### Try it

> **Exercise 4.4.1 — Highlight and decide.** Open a chapter you've written. Highlight every passage of description longer than two sentences.
>
> For each, apply the load-bearing-detail test: does this description tell the reader about the character, set something up later, or carry atmosphere the scene needs?
>
> If yes to one or more, leave it.
>
> If yes to none — cut to one sentence. Move on.
>
> Read the chapter back. The chapter has either gained pace, lost meaning, or both. If it gained pace and kept meaning, that's the lesson. If it lost meaning, restore *only* the descriptions that were doing the lost work — and only those.

## Quiz preview (5 questions, 70% to pass — applied register)

Drafted in the M1 widow-and-bees style. Each tests *understanding*, not memorisation.

**Q1.** A draft has a chapter where the protagonist's three-month grief is rendered scene by scene across forty pages. The pacing is slow; readers are losing patience. Per Lesson 1, what is the most likely diagnosis?
- (a) The grief isn't intense enough; the writer should add more emotional language.
- (b) The chapter is over-showing — three months of fictional time is a candidate for *telling*; the chapter could be a single paragraph that summarises the grief, with one or two key shown moments embedded in the summary. ✓
- (c) The chapter needs more dialogue.
- (d) The chapter should be cut entirely; grief has no place in fiction.

*Explanation:* (a) misreads the symptom. (c) is a workaround that doesn't address the cause. (d) overcorrects. (b) is L1's diagnosis: the writer is *showing* something that should be *told* — three months of similar emotional content is precisely what telling exists for. The cure is summary plus one or two key shown moments, not more showing.

**Q2.** A scene has been written with rich visual detail across two pages. Beta readers say the scene "feels flat" but can't say why. According to Lesson 2, what is most likely missing?
- (a) The scene needs more dialogue.
- (b) The scene engages only one sense — usually vision — and the reader experiences it as flat because real attention is multi-sensory; adding even one phrase from another sense (a smell, a sound) usually doubles the scene's grip without adding noticeable length. ✓
- (c) The scene is too long and should be cut.
- (d) The scene needs more characters.

*Explanation:* (a), (c), and (d) all guess at structural fixes. (b) is L2's specific diagnosis: the visual default is the most common reason a scene "feels flat." A single phrase from a second sense — *the faint smell of cigarette smoke that shouldn't have been there* — usually fixes the flatness without lengthening the scene meaningfully.

**Q3.** A novel's setting is described lavishly throughout — five sentences per room, weather noted at the start of every chapter. Per Lesson 3, what is most likely happening?
- (a) The setting is doing character work; the elevation is appropriate.
- (b) The world has become too detailed for the story to breathe inside it; the reader is reading a setting catalogue rather than a novel, and the cure is to identify which specific details are *load-bearing* and cut the rest. ✓
- (c) The setting needs even more detail to fully come alive.
- (d) The novel needs a more interesting protagonist.

*Explanation:* (a) is the writer's hope; (c) makes the failure worse; (d) misdirects. (b) is L3's diagnosis: setting elevation works through a few perfect details, not through pervasive lavishness. When every room gets five sentences, the *real* load-bearing rooms lose their weight — there's no contrast to mark them as special.

**Q4.** A writer's chapter opens with: "*The fog was thick, the city alive and watching, the streets a being unto themselves.*" Per Lesson 3's discussion of pretentiousness, what's the diagnostic move?
- (a) Make the prose more poetic — the writer is on the right track.
- (b) Replace the abstract claims with a single concrete detail that shows the same thing — for instance, *On the third day in the city, he noticed that the bus drivers all knew each other's first names, and none of them used his*. ✓
- (c) Cut the opening entirely; openings should be action-only.
- (d) Add more setting description to support the abstract claims.

*Explanation:* (a) doubles the failure. (c) overcorrects. (d) makes the failure worse. (b) is L3's specific cure: the failure mode of "setting as character" is the abstract claim; the fix is a concrete detail that does the same work without telling the reader what to feel.

**Q5.** A first draft is 130,000 words. Beta readers say the prose is "good but slow." The writer is unwilling to cut scenes. Per Module 4, what is the most efficient first cut?
- (a) Cut every scene by twenty percent uniformly.
- (b) Apply the load-bearing-detail test to all description: keep what tells the reader about the character, sets up later moments, or carries atmosphere; cut everything else, including descriptions the writer is fond of. ✓
- (c) Cut every chapter's first paragraph.
- (d) Cut all dialogue tags.

*Explanation:* (a) is unspecific and damages good scenes. (c) is a stylistic gimmick. (d) is irrelevant. (b) is L4's prescription, which the module has been building toward: the cure for "good but slow" first drafts is almost always to cut description that doesn't do work. The cuts feel like losses; they are concentrations. A 130,000-word draft that has had this pass usually loses 15,000–25,000 words and gains pace at every scale.
