## Cross-lesson arc

Module 3 moves *inside-out*. The student starts with the protagonist's interior, decides what the reader sees of it, then mirrors the framework onto the antagonist, surrounds them with cast, and finally brings everyone to the surface where they meet — dialogue.

| Lesson | Question it answers | What it leaves the student with |
|---|---|---|
| 1. The protagonist's wound and want | Who is this person at their core? | An event-level wound and a sentence-level want for their protagonist |
| 2. Backstory: how much is enough | What does the reader actually need to know about that core? | The 50 words of backstory that earn their place; the 450 that don't |
| 3. The antagonist (and why they need to be human) | Who opposes them, and why? | A paragraph from the antagonist's POV explaining why they're right |
| 4. Supporting cast | Who else is in the room, and which of them belong there? | A culled cast — every character justifying their page time |
| 5. Dialogue that sounds real | How do these people meet on the page? | A scene of subtextual dialogue between protagonist and antagonist |

By Module 3's end the student has: a core protagonist (wound + want), a controlled backstory iceberg, an antagonist with parallel architecture, a cast that earns its space, and one practiced scene where it all hits the page.

## Lesson 1: The protagonist's wound and want

**Slug:** `characters-protagonist-wound` · **Length target:** 1,300–1,800 words

### Thesis
A protagonist without a wound feels hollow even when their plot is exciting. The wound is the specific event that gave the character their want — and the want is what the reader is asked to invest in. Knowing both lets you write a character a reader will sit with for nine hours.

### Section structure

1. **The framework: wound → want → need** — *~250 words*
   - **Wound.** A specific event in the past. Not a "trauma" — a moment. Pip in *Great Expectations* is humiliated about his hands by Estella in a specific scene in a specific kitchen. The shame is not a generalised condition. It is *that afternoon*.
   - **Want.** What the character can name in a sentence. *I want her to think well of me. I want my brother back. I want to be left alone.* The want is on the surface. The character could say it out loud if asked.
   - **Need.** What the character actually requires for the story to resolve, which is rarely the same as the want. The character usually doesn't know their need yet.
   - The wound *causes* the want. The want *occludes* the need. Plot is the process by which the want is tested until the need becomes visible.

2. **Why protagonists without a wound feel hollow** — *~300 words*
   - A protagonist with a want and no wound is a list of preferences. The reader can track what they want; the reader cannot care that they get it. Without the wound, the want is decoration.
   - Compare two openings: *Sarah wants to win the regional bake-off* (preference). *Sarah wants to win the regional bake-off because her mother's last words were that she would never amount to anything in a kitchen* (wound + want). The second is six seconds older but a different book.
   - The wound is what *turns* the want into a stake.

3. **The wound is concrete** — *~250 words*
   - Stay specific. Not "abandonment issues" — *the morning his father packed a suitcase and didn't come back*. Not "she's afraid of failure" — *the gym teacher who laughed at her in front of the class when she was eleven*.
   - Therapy-speak generalises wounds because therapy is in the business of integration. Fiction is in the business of specificity. A wound that could fit any character is a wound that doesn't belong to *yours*.

4. **Wounds vs. trauma** — *~250 words*
   - Some wounds are large (a death, a war). Some are small (a humiliation, a betrayal in a kitchen). A small wound can carry a novel. A large wound can fail to carry a chapter.
   - The size of the event is not the size of the story. The size of the story is the *consequence* — what the character has built around the event in order to live with it. Pip's wound is small in the world's terms (a child humiliated by another child) and enormous in his — he spends three hundred pages building a costume of gentility around it.
   - Trauma-tourism — borrowing a vast historical pain to lend a character weight — is the failure mode here. The reader can tell. Use what you know specifically; let the consequence do the heavy lifting.

5. **When the wound becomes visible** — *~250 words*
   - The reader does not need the wound on page one. The reader needs the *want* on page one. The wound surfaces when the want is tested — usually around the midpoint, often at the dark moment (callback to M2 L1). The reader has been watching the protagonist pursue something, and at the midpoint they understand *why*.
   - The trick is to seed the wound in glances earlier — small reactions, moments of irrational tenderness or anger — so when the wound surfaces it confirms what the reader has been half-feeling.

6. **What this gives you** — *~150 words*
   - The mechanism of the *character* ingredient from M1 L4. Characters readers care about are characters whose want is rooted in a specific wound. The wound makes the want costly. The cost makes the journey real.

### Public-domain anchors
- *Great Expectations* (Dickens, 1861) — Pip's hands.
- *Wuthering Heights* (E. Brontë, 1847) — Heathcliff's rejection.
- *The Count of Monte Cristo* (Dumas, 1844–46) — Edmond Dantès's betrayal. Used once or twice; the wound (the cell, the false friend) and the want (vengeance) are textbook.
- *Jane Eyre* (C. Brontë, 1847) — the red room. Available as backup if Pip's example needs reinforcing.

### Try it

> **Exercise 3.1.1 — Find your protagonist's wound.** Take the protagonist from your premise (M1, Exercise 3.2). In 100 words, describe the *moment* their wound happened. Include: where they were, what specifically happened, what they did immediately afterward (often the most revealing detail).
>
> The moment may never appear in your book. That's fine. You need to know it exists before the first sentence.

## Lesson 2: Character backstory (how much is enough)

**Slug:** `characters-backstory` · **Length target:** 1,300–1,800 words

### Thesis
A writer who knows their character's backstory in detail writes a character with weight. A writer who *puts* all that backstory on the page writes a slow novel. The skill is the iceberg principle: know everything, show ten percent, choose which ten.

### Section structure

1. **The iceberg principle** — *~250 words*
   - Picture an iceberg. A small fraction is above water; the bulk is below. A character should feel that way to a reader: visible weight on the page, hidden mass underneath.
   - The mass is what *you* know about your character — the streets they grew up on, the marriage they've never spoken about, the dog. The visible part is what makes the page.
   - The ratio matters. Too little underneath and the character is a character-shaped hole. Too much on top and the reader is reading a dossier.

2. **Backstory dump vs. backstory revelation** — *~350 words*
   - **Dump:** the moment a writer interrupts the present-tense story to deliver a paragraph (or three pages, or a whole flashback chapter) of historical context. Reads as: *"Sarah had grown up in a house where..."* and never recovers momentum.
   - **Revelation:** a single sentence, dropped in dialogue or narrated thought, that pivots the reader's understanding of what's happening *right now*. Three words can do the work of three pages.
   - The difference is direction. A dump moves the reader *backward* in time. A revelation pulls the past *forward* into the present moment, where it changes the meaning of what the character just said or did.

3. **The load-bearing test** — *~300 words*
   - Take any line of backstory in your draft. Ask one question: *Does this explain a present-day choice the character is making?*
   - If yes: keep it.
   - If no: cut it.
   - The test is brutal because most of what writers love about their character lore fails it. The childhood dog, the school friend, the favourite teacher — wonderful in your notes; in the manuscript, unless one of them is causing a present-day choice, deadweight.
   - Backstory earns its way onto the page by being *causal*, not by being interesting.

4. **When to reveal vs. when to withhold** — *~250 words*
   - Withhold when the reader's curiosity is *productive*. A reader wondering why your protagonist hates a certain word is a reader engaged.
   - Reveal when the *not knowing* becomes a wall between the reader and the character. There comes a point where mystery turns into evasion, and the reader's investment cools.
   - Most beginners over-reveal in Act 1 and under-reveal in Acts 2 and 3. The reverse is the right rhythm: hold most of the backstory in reserve until the want is being seriously tested, then release it where it pays the most. Backstory revelations land hardest at the midpoint (callback to [M2 L1](/learn/lesson/architecture-three-acts)) and at the dark moment.

5. **Common backstory mistakes** — *~250 words*
   - **The opening flashback.** Five pages of childhood before the reader has any reason to care. Cut.
   - **The "as you know" dialogue.** Two characters explain things to each other that they both already know, for the reader's benefit. *"As you know, Mark, our father died in the fire of 1872."* Cut and rewrite.
   - **The retrofitted reveal.** A backstory invented in Act 3 to justify the climax. Readers can smell this. Make sure your wound (L1) is in place from page one, even if the reader doesn't see it yet.

6. **What this gives you** — *~150 words*
   - The instinct to know your character at iceberg-depth and to write them at iceberg-surface.
   - A test for every line of backstory in your draft.
   - The exercise asks you to write five hundred words of backstory and keep fifty. The four hundred and fifty you cut don't disappear — they live in your notes, doing weight-work without taking up reader-space.

### Public-domain anchors
- *Jane Eyre* (C. Brontë, 1847) — the red room is two pages of present-tense narration that contains all the backstory the rest of the novel needs about Jane's emotional architecture. A textbook iceberg-tip.
- *Anna Karenina* (Tolstoy, 1877) — Levin's family backstory is dropped across the novel in single-sentence revelations that always pivot a present-day scene.
- *Great Expectations* (Dickens, 1861) — for contrast: a novel that *does* dump backstory in places, and the places it sags are exactly those.

### Try it

> **Exercise 3.2.1 — Write 500, keep 50.** Take your protagonist. Write 500 words of backstory in any form: a list, a paragraph, scattered notes. Their family. The house. The thing they don't talk about. Childhood pets. A teacher. A first failure.
>
> Then identify the 50 words a reader of your novel actually *needs* to know — not what would be nice for them to know, but what would change a present-day choice if they didn't.
>
> Cut the other 450 words from your manuscript permanently. Keep them in your notes. They are still doing work — they make the 50 you keep feel weighted. The reader can feel the iceberg even when they only see the tip.

## Lesson 3: The antagonist (and why they need to be human)

**Slug:** `characters-antagonist` · **Length target:** 1,400–1,900 words

### Thesis
An antagonist isn't a villain. A villain is *evil*. An antagonist is whoever or whatever stands between the protagonist and their want — and the strongest antagonists have their own wound, their own want, and a reason a reader could understand even if they can't endorse it. Default to humanising your antagonist. Reach for genuine evil only when you can earn it.

### Section structure

1. **Antagonist ≠ villain** — *~200 words*
   - An antagonist *opposes*. A villain *is evil*. Most antagonists are not villains. Most are people whose want collides with the protagonist's.
   - Mr. Wickham in *Pride and Prejudice* opposes Elizabeth's want (to be seen accurately) without being evil — he is charming, self-interested, and not particularly cruel. He works as an antagonist because his want (a comfortable life) is in direct contradiction with hers, and his methods (deception) are something Elizabeth has to learn to read.
   - The distinction matters because it changes what you're building. A villain is a moral problem. An antagonist is a structural problem with the protagonist's path.

2. **Mirror the protagonist's framework** — *~350 words*
   - Whatever you did for your protagonist in [Lesson 1](/learn/lesson/characters-protagonist-wound), do for your antagonist.
   - **Wound.** What specific past event made them this person? The cousin who inherited what should have been theirs. The system that promoted someone less qualified. The friend who chose someone else.
   - **Want.** What can they name in a sentence? *I want her to fail. I want my position back. I want to be the one who tells the truth about what happened.*
   - **Need.** Almost certainly different from their want. The antagonist usually doesn't see their need any more clearly than the protagonist sees theirs.
   - The closer your antagonist's wound and want come to *paralleling* your protagonist's, the more dangerous they become. The strongest antagonists are people who could have been the protagonist of a different book — and who, in this book, want what the protagonist wants for reasons they can name.

3. **"They think they're the hero"** — *~300 words*
   - The line gets quoted to death because it's true. Most people in conflict believe themselves justified. The antagonist who can be made to articulate their justification — *out loud, in their own voice, without irony* — is the antagonist who can carry a novel.
   - This is not a moral claim. It's an observational one. Even a genuinely cruel person believes their cruelty is reasonable in context. The novelist's job is to find the context, not to endorse it.
   - The exercise at the end of the lesson is the practical version: write a paragraph from your antagonist's point of view, sympathetic to themselves, where they explain why they are right. If you can't, your antagonist isn't ready. They are not a person yet — they are a function.

4. **When evil is the right choice** — *~300 words*
   - Genuine evil — the antagonist whose motives don't reduce to a comprehensible want — exists in literature. Iago in *Othello* is the canonical example. He delivers several speeches attempting to explain his hatred of Othello, and none of them quite hold together. The play knows this and uses it: Iago is not a man whose want we are meant to understand. He is a force with a face.
   - Evil works as a choice when:
     - The story is mythic in scale and the antagonist is a *force* the protagonist must survive.
     - The point of the story is the protagonist's confrontation with something inexplicable.
     - The writer can hold the evil at the right distance — close enough to feel, far enough that the reader doesn't try to diagnose it.
   - Evil fails when it's used as a shortcut. *He's evil because he's evil* is the antagonist version of *she's strong because she's strong*. The reader sees through both immediately.
   - Default: humanise. Reach for evil only when you can earn it.

5. **The system as antagonist** — *~250 words*
   - Sometimes the antagonist isn't a person. Poverty. War. An illness. An institution.
   - System-as-antagonist works because it gives the protagonist an obstacle that cannot be reasoned with — the obstacle that forces the deepest internal change (callback to [M1 L4](/learn/lesson/foundation-three-ingredients)).
   - The risk: a system can be too abstract to fight. Most great novels with system antagonists *also* have a human face for the system somewhere — a specific officer, a specific landlord, a specific bureaucrat. The system carries the weight; the person makes it concrete enough to confront.

6. **What this gives you** — *~200 words*
   - A second character with the same architecture as your protagonist. Wound, want, need.
   - A test: can you write a paragraph from the antagonist's point of view in which they sound *right*? If not, more work is needed.
   - The conflict ingredient from M1 L4 gets sharper here: a specific obstacle is much more specific when it has wants of its own.

### Public-domain anchors
- *Othello* (Shakespeare, ~1603) — Iago, for genuine-evil-done-well.
- *Pride and Prejudice* (Austen, 1813) — Mr. Wickham, for the charming antagonist with his own logic.
- *Les Misérables* (Hugo, 1862) — Inspector Javert, for the principled antagonist (a man whose moral framework forces him to oppose someone the reader is rooting for).
- *Frankenstein* (Shelley, 1818) — for the antagonist-question itself: who is the antagonist of the book, Victor or his creature?

### Try it

> **Exercise 3.3.1 — The antagonist's case.** Take your antagonist. Write a single paragraph from their point of view, sympathetic to themselves, in which they explain why they are *right*. No irony. No authorial commentary. Just the antagonist talking.
>
> If you cannot write the paragraph, your antagonist isn't ready. You have a function, not a person.
>
> If you can — read it once and ask whether your protagonist's want, in light of what the antagonist just said, still feels worth a novel. If yes, the conflict is in good shape. If no, that's information about which of the two characters needs more work.

## Lesson 4: Supporting cast

**Slug:** `characters-supporting-cast` · **Length target:** 1,200–1,600 words

### Thesis
Every supporting character earns their page time or steals it. The cull is brutal because most beginners' drafts are full of characters who *would* be doing work if the writer asked them to — but the writer hasn't. This lesson teaches you to ask.

### Section structure

1. **Functional vs. dimensional** — *~250 words*
   - A **functional** supporting character does a single job in the story: the mentor who delivers the tool, the rival who applies pressure, the confidant who lets the protagonist speak their thoughts aloud.
   - A **dimensional** supporting character does the function plus has *their own* visible life. The mentor who is also tired of being asked. The rival who is genuinely surprised when they win. The confidant who is, secretly, in love.
   - Most supporting characters should be functional. A novel with twelve dimensional characters reads like an ensemble drama — sometimes the right choice, often a mess.
   - The skill is knowing which characters deserve the second dimension and which are overstaying their welcome.

2. **The two-trait rule** — *~250 words*
   - A supporting character with one trait is cardboard.
   - The brave knight. The stern father. The wise old woman.
   - A supporting character with two traits is a person.
   - The brave knight who is also a coward about heights. The stern father who keeps a child's drawing in his coat pocket. The wise old woman who is wrong, sometimes, on purpose, because she likes to be argued with.
   - Two specifically *unrelated* traits. *Brave and noble* is one trait pretending to be two. *Brave and afraid of heights* is two, because they create a small contradiction the reader's mind has to hold.
   - The two-trait rule isn't a recipe. It's a minimum. It's the line between cardboard and person.

3. **The classical roles, used carefully** — *~300 words*
   - **The mentor.** Gives the protagonist a tool, knowledge, or permission to act. Worked best when also a person with their own life and their own losses. Worked worst when a wisdom-dispenser without a body.
   - **The foil.** Defined by contrast with the protagonist. Shows the reader who the protagonist is by being *almost* like them but not quite. Mr. Bennet in *Pride and Prejudice* is a foil to Elizabeth — same intellect, different ethical compass — and the contrast is one of the things that makes Elizabeth's choices feel earned.
   - **The confidant.** Lets the protagonist speak their thoughts aloud without it sounding like internal monologue. Worked best when the confidant *pushes back* — confidants who only listen are, again, function-shaped.
   - **The rival.** Wants something the protagonist wants. Different from the antagonist (whose want directly opposes the protagonist's); the rival is in parallel pursuit. A rival is often more useful than an antagonist for stories about ambition.
   - These roles are tools. Naming them shouldn't be a substitute for writing the character.

4. **When to cut, when to merge** — *~300 words*
   - The cut question: *what would the story lose if I removed this character?* If the answer is "nothing", cut them. If the answer is "another character would have to do their job", consider merging.
   - Two minor characters with overlapping functions are almost always one minor character. The reader's working memory has limits — a chapter with eleven named characters performs worse than the same chapter with seven.
   - When you merge, the character that emerges is usually richer than either of the originals. Combine the mentor and the rival into one figure and you get a much more interesting kind of help — guidance from someone with their own stake in the outcome.

5. **Don't make a cardboard ally to your real protagonist** — *~150 words*
   - A real protagonist (with the wound and want from L1) surrounded by cardboard *demotes* the protagonist. The reader feels the imbalance. If everyone else is a function, the protagonist becomes a function-shaped person too.
   - Same applies to your antagonist (L3). A real antagonist with cardboard henchmen is a real antagonist who looks like they're acting alone in a populated world.

6. **What this gives you** — *~150 words*
   - A diagnostic for every secondary character in your draft.
   - A way to merge or cut without losing structural function.
   - A practical link to [M2 L4](/learn/lesson/architecture-choosing-structure): supporting cast is in service of the structure you chose. Characters whose function is unclear are usually structural patches in disguise.

### Public-domain anchors
- *Pride and Prejudice* (Austen, 1813) — Mrs. Bennet (function: pressure on Elizabeth) and Mr. Bennet (function: foil + confidant). Two-trait rule: Mr. Bennet is witty *and* derelict; Mrs. Bennet is anxious *and* shrewd in marriage-economics.
- *The Pickwick Papers* (Dickens, 1836–37) — Sam Weller, the savvy servant. Functional role with two traits (loyal *and* cynical) that make him outlive most of the cast.
- *Macbeth* (Shakespeare, ~1606) — Banquo, the moral mirror.

### Try it

> **Exercise 3.4.1 — Cull the cast.** List every supporting character in your premise (M1, Exercise 3.2). For each, answer two questions:
>
> 1. What is their *function* — the single job they do for the story?
> 2. What is their *second* trait — the one unrelated to their function that turns them from cardboard into a person?
>
> If you cannot answer (1), the character is not yet doing work. Cut, or give them a job.
>
> If you cannot answer (2), the character is cardboard. Add a second trait or accept that they're a function-only role and write them sparely.
>
> If two characters share the same answer to (1), consider merging them.

## Lesson 5: Dialogue that sounds real

**Slug:** `characters-dialogue` · **Length target:** 1,400–1,900 words

### Thesis
Real speech is messy: ums, repetitions, half-finished thoughts. Fictional speech is *real-sounding*, not real. The work is to write dialogue that feels true to a reader's ear while doing something on the page that real talk almost never does — carrying the story forward in every line.

### Section structure

> **Note for Phase B drafting:** this lesson must *demonstrate* voice differentiation in inline dialogue snippets, not just describe it. Two short invented exchanges will do most of the teaching weight — one showing distinct voices side by side, one showing subtext where neither character says what they mean. Both exchanges will be fully invented (no PD-derivative concerns).

1. **Real speech vs. real-sounding speech** — *~250 words*
   - Transcribe two minutes of any conversation. The transcript is unreadable. *"Yeah no I mean — well, the thing is — what I was — hold on, where did I —"*. Real speech is mostly throat-clearing.
   - Fictional dialogue strips out the throat-clearing while keeping the *texture*. A character can pause, can interrupt themselves, can repeat — but every preserved imperfection is doing work. It is signalling state, not transcribing reality.
   - The test: does this hesitation tell the reader something about the character or the moment? If yes, keep. If no, cut.

2. **Voice differentiation: a short demonstration** — *~300 words*
   - **Inline dialogue example here in Phase B.** Two characters speaking back-to-back, one terse and literal, one verbose and metaphorical, on the same topic. The point is to *show* the gap rather than explain it. A single page of dialogue where the reader could identify the speaker even with the tags removed is voice differentiation working.
   - The principle: a character's voice is downstream of their *wound* (callback to L1). What they protect, what they avoid, what they reach for verbally — all of these track to the thing the wound made them this way to defend. Two characters with the same wound speak similarly. Two characters with different wounds speak differently, even when their formal education is the same.
   - The trap: differentiation by *quirk* (a character who always says *bloody hell*; a character with a stutter). Quirks are surface decoration. The reader notices once and then ignores. Real voice differentiation is structural — sentence length, what the character *avoids* talking about, the level of abstraction they prefer.

3. **Subtext: a short demonstration** — *~350 words*
   - **Second inline dialogue example in Phase B.** A short exchange in which both characters know what they're really arguing about, neither says it directly, and the reader can feel both. The point is to teach by showing what subtext looks like on the page.
   - The principle: most important conversations don't happen on the surface. People talk *around* the thing. They argue about the dishes when they are afraid of leaving each other.
   - For the writer, this is an enormous gift. Surface dialogue can do exposition while subtext does the emotional work. The reader gets both at once.
   - The classic public-domain example is the long conversation in Hemingway's *Hills Like White Elephants* (1927; entered US PD in 2023), in which a couple talks about a procedure neither of them ever names. The whole story is what isn't said.

4. **Tags, beats, and what to use when** — *~250 words*
   - **Tags.** *Said* and *asked* are invisible. They do their job and disappear. *Whispered, muttered, exclaimed, growled* — used sparingly. Once or twice per scene, when the manner of speaking matters and isn't visible from the words alone.
   - **Action beats.** Physical movement that breaks dialogue and signals who is speaking. *She set the cup down. "I don't think you mean that."* These do triple duty — attribution, pacing, character. Most beats are better than most tags.
   - The general rule: *said* by default; an action beat when a tag would be a missed opportunity; a non-said tag only when nothing else will carry the weight.

5. **When to write the long monologue and when to cut it** — *~250 words*
   - A long monologue earns its place when the speaker is doing something *only* a long monologue can do: confessing, deceiving, performing for an audience, working out a thought in real time on the page.
   - A long monologue fails when it's exposition with quotation marks. *"As you know, Mark, our family has had this house since 1872..."*
   - The test: could this monologue be cut to four lines? If yes, cut it. The remainder is what the monologue was *actually* doing.

6. **What this gives you** — *~150 words*
   - A way to write dialogue that sounds real without being a transcript.
   - Voice differentiation rooted in character architecture (L1) rather than surface quirk.
   - A technique — subtext — that lets the reader experience two layers of meaning at once.
   - The next module (Module 4) builds the *world* this dialogue happens inside. A line of dialogue is partly the speaker, partly the room they're standing in.

### Public-domain anchors
- *Hills Like White Elephants* (Hemingway, 1927; PD in US since 2023). The reference point for subtext.
- *The Importance of Being Earnest* (Wilde, 1895). Voice differentiation as comedic engine — Algernon and Jack speak the same dialect but in opposite registers.
- *Othello* (Shakespeare, ~1603). Iago, Othello, and Desdemona all speak verse, but Iago's is full of negation and commerce, Othello's is full of weather and battle, Desdemona's is full of negotiation. Same medium, three voices.
- *Hard Times* (Dickens, 1854). Sleary's lisp is a quirk, but Bounderby's bluster, Gradgrind's pedantry, and Stephen's cadence are voice in the structural sense.

### Try it

> **Exercise 3.5.1 — Subtext under pressure.** Write a one-page conversation between your protagonist and your antagonist (from L3) in which neither says what they actually mean. The reader should be able to feel what each really wants. Neither character should *voice* it directly.
>
> Constraints:
> - At least eight exchanges.
> - At least one action beat per character.
> - No internal monologue. No narration explaining what they really feel. The reader gets only what's on the page — words and action.
>
> If you can do this once, you've done one of the hardest things in fiction. If the page reads as on-the-nose (each character voicing their actual want), revise once: cut the line where they say it directly and let the surrounding lines bear the weight.

## Quiz preview (5 questions, 70% to pass — applied register)

Drafted in the M1 widow-and-bees style. Each tests *understanding*, not memorisation.

**Q1.** Which of the following best matches the wound/want/need framework from Lesson 1?
- (a) The wound is what the character feels generally; the want is their preference; the need is their goal.
- (b) The wound is a specific past event; the want is what the character can name in a sentence; the need is what they actually require for the story to resolve, which is rarely the same as the want. ✓
- (c) The wound is whatever the protagonist has trauma about; the want is the antagonist's goal; the need is the climax.
- (d) The wound, the want, and the need are three names for the same thing — what motivates the protagonist.

*Explanation:* Lesson 1's framework is precise. (a) generalises the wound (it's a *moment*, not a feeling). (c) garbles the antagonist's role. (d) collapses three distinct concepts. Only (b) keeps the three layered: an event, a sentence-level pursuit, and an underlying requirement the character usually doesn't see.

**Q2.** A writer's draft opens with a five-page flashback to the protagonist's childhood, intended to give the reader context for the present-day chapter that follows. According to Lesson 2, what's most likely happening?
- (a) The opening is good — readers need context before they can care about a protagonist.
- (b) The opening is a backstory dump that moves the reader backward in time before they have a reason to care; the same context could likely land in single-sentence revelations across the first three chapters. ✓
- (c) The opening should be longer to give more context.
- (d) The opening should be cut entirely; backstory has no place in fiction.

*Explanation:* (a) misreads how readers form investment — care comes from the *want* on page one, not from background. (c) makes the failure mode worse. (d) overcorrects. (b) is the L2 diagnosis: backstory dumps move the reader backward before they're invested; the cure is revelation, not removal.

**Q3.** A writer drafts an antagonist whose only motivation is "she wants to ruin Sarah's life because she's jealous." Per Lesson 3, what is the most useful next step?
- (a) Add more obstacles in the antagonist's way to make her seem stronger.
- (b) Make the antagonist evil instead of merely jealous, since evil is a stronger motive.
- (c) Build the antagonist's wound and want with the same tools you used for Sarah, then write a paragraph from her point of view in which she explains why she is right. ✓
- (d) Cut the antagonist; an unsympathetic antagonist hurts the book.

*Explanation:* (a) addresses a different problem (obstacle weakness, not character depth). (b) misreads when evil works — evil is a choice for mythic-scale antagonists, not a default upgrade. (d) is wrong; the protagonist needs an opponent. (c) is L3's specific instruction: mirror the protagonist's framework on the antagonist, then test by writing the antagonist's case in their own voice.

**Q4.** A draft has fourteen named supporting characters. Two of them — a quirky bookshop owner and a quirky archivist — both deliver historical research to the protagonist at separate points. Per Lesson 4, what is the most useful move?
- (a) Add more distinguishing traits to each so readers can tell them apart.
- (b) Cut both and have the protagonist do the research themselves.
- (c) Merge them into one character whose function is "researcher who helps the protagonist," then give that single character two unrelated traits to make them dimensional. ✓
- (d) Keep both; supporting cast is supposed to feel populated.

*Explanation:* (a) treats the symptom (confusion) without addressing the cause (redundant function). (b) loses a useful structural role. (d) ignores the cognitive load problem. (c) is L4's prescription: same function = merge candidates; the merger ends up richer than either original.

**Q5.** A writer reads back a scene of dialogue between their protagonist and antagonist. Both characters' lines could be spoken by the other without changing meaning. Per Lesson 5, what is most likely happening, and what's the diagnostic move?
- (a) The scene is well-balanced; matching dialogue between rivals is a sign of equal stature.
- (b) Voice differentiation is failing because the two characters are not yet rooted in distinct *wounds* — voice rises from what each character protects, and undifferentiated voices usually mean undifferentiated interiors. ✓
- (c) The scene needs more dialogue tags so the reader can tell who's speaking.
- (d) The scene needs more quirks (a stutter, a catchphrase) for each character.

*Explanation:* (a) misreads "balanced" as equivalence. (c) is a workaround that papers over the root cause. (d) confuses surface decoration with structural voice — quirks the reader notices once and ignores. (b) is L5's diagnosis: real voice differentiation is downstream of distinct interior architecture (wound, want), and the cure is in the character work, not in the dialogue itself.
