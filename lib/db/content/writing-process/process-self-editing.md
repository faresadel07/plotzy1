# Self-editing checklist

A checklist that doesn't tell you *why* each item matters is a checklist you can't adapt.

A writer who knows that *use vivid verbs* is on the list but doesn't know *why* will follow the rule on every page, including the pages where a flat verb is doing better work. A writer who knows the *why* applies the rule when it serves the prose and breaks it when it doesn't.

The rest of this lesson is a working checklist. About twenty items, organised by Pass (callback to [Lesson 3](/learn/lesson/process-revision-passes)). Every item is a yes/no question. Every item gets one sentence on why it matters.

## A checklist organised by pass

Three sections (Structural, Scene, Line) matching the three passes from L3. The order matters; structural items get checked first, line items last. Polishing prose in a chapter you'll cut is the failure mode the previous lesson warned you about.

The checklist is not exhaustive. It is *load-bearing*. Every item earns its place by catching a specific problem the earlier modules taught you to recognise. Most items are diagnostic in disguise: a question that, if you answer no, points to a specific fix.

:::check
Q: Why does every item on this checklist come with a *why*?
- So the list is easier to memorise
-* So you can adapt each rule to your work, and break it where it serves the prose
- So you can justify your edits to beta readers
X: A writer who knows only the rule follows it on every page, including pages where breaking it does better work. Knowing the why is what makes the rule adaptable.
:::

## Pass 1: structural

:::checklist
- Is the protagonist's want clear by chapter 2 or page 30? Readers form their investment from the want; without it they don't yet care what happens.
- Is there a clear inciting incident in Act 1? The disturbance to the normal is what makes Act 2 necessary; without it, the story keeps introducing.
- Is there a lock-in by the end of Act 1? Without a choice the protagonist cannot un-make, Act 2 has nothing to start from.
- Is there a midpoint reversal at roughly the middle? A sagging middle is almost always a missing midpoint.
- Is there a dark moment late in Act 2? The climax has nothing to climb out of without a floor in Act 2.
- Does the protagonist change by the end? A story whose situation changed but whose character didn't is a complaint, not a story.
- Does every act break feel earned? The reader should feel the story arrived here, not the writer needed an act break here.
:::

## Pass 2: scene-level

:::checklist
- Does this scene have the three ingredients? Without want, obstacle, and change it doesn't function as a unit of narrative; the reader senses filler.
- Does the scene start late and end early? Cutting the first and last paragraph of a draft scene usually improves it.
- Are the right characters present? A scene with two characters who could be one is a scene that hasn't been edited yet.
- Is the conflict in the scene specific, not generic? "Things go badly" is not a conflict; a specific want meeting a specific obstacle is.
- Does the scene either advance the plot or change the protagonist? If neither, the scene is filler, even if the prose is good.
- Is the setting doing work? The *where* should pull weight, or the location is decoration the reader will skim over.
:::

## Pass 3: line-level

:::checklist
- Are dialogue tags mostly *said*? The eye trips on *exclaimed* and *muttered*; it does not trip on *said*. Variation reads as nervous; *said* reads as transparent.
- Are there fewer than three adverbs per page? An adverb often signals a verb that wasn't strong enough; the fix is usually a stronger verb.
- Are there fewer than two adjectives in a row? *The cold, dark, empty room* should be one of those words; pick the one that does the most work.
- Is the same word repeated within two paragraphs accidentally? Deliberate repetition works for emphasis; accidental repetition reads as carelessness.
- Are sentences varied in length? All-similar-length sentences read as monotonous; the ear hears the rhythm even when the reader doesn't notice.
- Is description load-bearing? Description that doesn't reveal character, set up something later, or carry needed atmosphere is cut.
- Does each shown scene engage at least two senses? A scene with only sight engaged feels flat to the reader even when they can't say why.
- Have you read this aloud? The ear catches what the eye doesn't; the most reliable single line-level diagnostic.
:::

## The "out loud" test

Most writers don't read aloud because it feels strange.

Read aloud anyway.

The strangeness is the lesson. Sentences your eye accepts your tongue refuses. A paragraph that scans well silently can have a clause buried in it that catches the breath halfway through. Out loud, you find it.

You don't need to read the whole manuscript aloud (that takes weeks). Read the chapters that feel slightly off. Read the scenes you're unsure about. Read the dialogue.

A reading-aloud session that lasts thirty minutes catches more line-level problems than three hours of silent re-reading.

## When to stop checking

The checklist is for one revision pass through the draft. Not for every paragraph forever.

After one full line-level pass with the checklist, stop.

The next lesson (*when to stop revising entirely*) is the deeper version of this question. The signal that you've over-applied the checklist is that you're moving the same fixes back and forth between passes: putting an adverb in, taking it out, putting it back. That's the curve telling you to stop.

## What this gives you

A working checklist organised by pass.

A reason for each item, so you can adapt the rule to your work, or break it where it serves the prose.

The discipline to apply the checklist *once* per pass, not forever.

The next lesson closes the module: how to know when the work is done.

## Try it

:::exercise
**One chapter, line-level pass.** Take a chapter you've already revised. Run it through the line-level checklist above, ticking each item as you go.

List the fixes you find here: places where dialogue tags are wrong, places where description doesn't earn its keep, places where adverbs could be cut.

The list is information about your *blind spots*. Most writers consistently miss the same two or three categories. Knowing which two or three is what lets you self-edit faster on the next book.
:::

:::takeaway
- A checklist without a why for each item is a checklist you cannot adapt; the why is what lets you break the rule where it serves the prose.
- Check in pass order: structural first, scene next, line last, so you never polish prose in a chapter you will cut.
- Every item is a yes/no question, and a no points to a specific fix.
- Reading aloud is the single most reliable line-level diagnostic; thirty minutes out loud beats three hours of silent re-reading.
- Apply the checklist once per pass, not forever; shuffling the same fixes back and forth is the signal to stop.
:::
