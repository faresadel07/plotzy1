# The blank page (overcoming it)

Sitting and not writing is not the same as choosing not to write.

The fear is real. The perfectionism is real. The voice that says *anything you put down will be worse than nothing* is real.

This lesson is about working through it. Mechanically. Not with pep talks.

## The blank page is real, but "writer's block" is the wrong name

The phrase *writer's block* hides the diagnosis.

Most blank pages are one of four specific things. Naming the cause is half the cure.

**Fear of being bad.** You are protecting yourself from producing something embarrassing. The worst sentence on the page is worse than the *idea* of the best sentence in your head, so you keep the page blank.

**Perfectionism.** You are waiting for the right sentence before starting. The right sentence does not arrive on a blank page. It arrives on the third bad sentence.

**Unclear premise.** You cannot draft a story whose engine you haven't found yet. Callback to [Module 1, Lesson 3](/learn/lesson/foundation-finding-idea): when the blank page is *information* about the premise, the cure is at premise level, not at typing level.

**No plan.** You don't know what *this scene* is supposed to do. You're trying to draft without knowing the function.

Each cause has a different fix. The next sections are the three tactics that work for the first two, and the test for when the cause is the third or fourth.

## Three tactics that work

**Start in the middle of a scene.**

Skip the throat-clearing. The first paragraph of any chapter usually gets cut anyway; write the second paragraph first, where the scene is already moving.

> He set the cup down and said *I told you I would* before she finished asking the question.

You're already in. The paragraph before this one (wherever they were, however he came in) can be filled in later or never. The important sentence is the one where something is happening.

**Write the easy scene first.**

The scene you've been imagining for months, the one you can already see in your head, is not necessarily chapter one. That's fine.

Drafting in order is a convention, not a rule. The momentum from a scene you wrote well will carry you into the harder ones. Most beginner writers waste two months trying to write a difficult opening because the opening "should" come first; the opening can be written in the third week of drafting, after the easy scenes have built the writer's confidence in the project.

**Write a terrible first paragraph on purpose.**

Aim for bad. Compete with yourself for the worst opening sentence you can produce. *The morning was a morning, and Sarah was Sarah, and she did the thing that Saturdays make a person do.* Worse than that, if you can.

:::quote
The fear of being bad cannot survive the requirement to be bad.
:::

Once the page is no longer blank, the second paragraph is a different problem.

## Constraints help

A blank page in front of unlimited time is harder than a blank page with a 25-minute timer.

**The timer.** Twenty-five minutes is short enough that the inner critic doesn't fully wake up. Longer sessions are useful; the critic learns to talk over them.

**The minimum word count.** Most writers do better with a small daily target they cannot fail (250 words) than a large one they sometimes hit (2,000). The target is for the *habit*; the habit is for the book. The next lesson is about that habit.

**The skipped chapter.** If the chapter you're supposed to write next won't come, write the chapter after it. Mark the missing chapter with a placeholder. Come back when the surrounding pages have told you what the missing one needs to do.

## When the blank page is telling you something

Sometimes you are not blocked. Sometimes the premise isn't ready, or the scene doesn't have a function yet.

Sign: when you sit down to write the *same* scene three days in a row and produce nothing each time, the issue is rarely fear. The issue is usually that the scene doesn't know what it's for.

Cure: leave the page. Go back to outline level. Ask of the scene: *what does the protagonist want here, what's in the way, what changes by the end?* (Callback to [Module 1, Lesson 4](/learn/lesson/foundation-three-ingredients): the three ingredients aren't only for the whole novel; every scene is a small story with the same architecture.) When you can answer those three questions, the scene will write itself in twenty minutes.

The blank page is not always a failure of will. Sometimes it's the writer's instinct telling you the work isn't ready. Listen for that.

## What this gives you

The blank page is a real problem. It is not unsolvable. The next lesson is about the longer version of the same question: once you have started, how do you keep going for six months.

## Try it

:::exercise
**Exercise 5.1.1: The 25-minute, 500-word session.** Set a timer for twenty-five minutes. Open a fresh document. Write 500 words on any scene from your story.

Rules:

- Do not stop typing.
- Do not edit while writing.
- Allowed: rambling, repetition, confusion, *[notes to self in brackets]*, restarting the same sentence three times.

The session does not produce *publishable* prose. It produces *prose*. That's what you need.

If you produce 500 words, the page is no longer blank. If you produce 200 words, the page is no longer blank. Tomorrow's session is easier. The lesson is mechanical.
:::

:::takeaway
- Most blank pages have one of four causes: fear of being bad, perfectionism, an unclear premise, or no plan for the scene. Name the cause first.
- Fear and perfectionism respond to tactics: start in the middle of a scene, write the easy scene first, or write a terrible paragraph on purpose.
- Constraints beat willpower: a 25-minute timer and a small unfailable word count outwork unlimited time.
- If the same scene fails three days running, leave the page and fix the scene's function at outline level.
:::

## Further reading

- *An Autobiography* (Anthony Trollope, 1883). Brief, direct, public-domain. Trollope wrote two hundred and fifty words every quarter-hour before walking to his job at the Post Office, and produced forty-seven novels by the end of his life. The chapter on his daily routine is the disenchantment of *blocked writer* as a category. Once you've read it, the word *blocked* never quite means the same thing again.
