# Revision in 3 passes

You cannot fix a draft in one pass.

Structural problems and line-level problems live at different scales. Trying to see both at once is why writers spend three months polishing prose in chapters they will eventually cut.

The cure is sequence. Three passes — structural, scene, line — in that order. The sequence is the lesson; the rest of this module is what you do during each pass.

## Why one pass doesn't work

You cannot simultaneously hold *does this chapter belong in the book* and *is this comma in the right place*.

They are different mental modes.

Structural mode is loose, abstract, willing to discard scenes. It looks at the manuscript as a *shape*. It reads fast.

Line mode is tight, specific, in love with the sentence. It looks at the manuscript as *prose*. It reads slow.

Run them at the same time and one wins. Usually line mode wins, because line mode produces visible progress (this paragraph is better!) while structural mode produces invisible progress (this chapter is gone).

The cost of running them simultaneously is wasted polish.

A perfectly worded paragraph in a chapter that will be cut is wasted work. Six months of wasted work is how a draft dies in revision.

**Don't fix sentences in chapters you'll cut.** This is the spine of the lesson. Every other thing in this module is downstream of it.

## Pass 1: structural

One question: *does the story work?*

Read the manuscript fast. The pace of the read matters — read it the way a stranger would, in three or four sittings, not over six weeks of careful study. You want the *shape* of the book, not the texture.

Take notes only on what isn't working at the chapter scale:

- Scenes that don't belong.
- Characters whose function is unclear.
- Structural beats that are missing or weak.
- Pacing problems at the act scale (sagging middle, rushed ending).

**Tool:** a single sheet of paper. One sentence per scene, summarising what that scene *does* for the story. If you cannot write the sentence, the scene isn't doing work.

The output of Pass 1 is a *list of structural changes*. Add a chapter. Cut a chapter. Move a chapter from after the midpoint to before it. Replace the antagonist's first appearance with a stronger one.

Don't fix anything yet. Write the list.

Then make the changes. Add the missing chapters. Cut the dead ones. Move what needs moving. Rewrite where needed *at the structural level only* — meaning rough scenes, not polished prose. The sentences will be terrible. That is correct. They are placeholders for Pass 2 to inhabit.

At the end of Pass 1 you have a draft whose architecture is sound. The prose may be awful in the chapters you rewrote. That's expected.

## Pass 2: scene-level

The question for each scene: *does this scene earn its place?*

A scene earns its place if it has the three ingredients from [Module 1](/learn/lesson/foundation-three-ingredients). Someone wants something. Something is in the way. Something has changed by the end.

Read the manuscript again, scene by scene. For each scene that does not have the three ingredients clearly, mark it. Either rewrite the scene to have them, or cut it.

Other Pass 2 checks:

- Does the scene start late and end early? Most beginner scenes start a paragraph too early and end a paragraph too late.
- Does at least one of the major structural beats from [Module 2](/learn/lesson/architecture-three-acts) — inciting incident, lock-in, midpoint, dark moment, climax — live in this scene? Not every scene; but the right scenes need to.
- Are the right characters present? Could two characters be merged? (Callback to [Module 3, Lesson 4](/learn/lesson/characters-supporting-cast).)
- Is the conflict in the scene specific, not generic?

Pass 2 produces scenes that work as scenes, in a manuscript whose structure is already sound.

The prose is *still* not polished. Pass 3 is where polish happens. Resist the urge to polish a sentence you encounter on Pass 2 — even when the fix is obvious. A polished sentence in a scene Pass 3 will further revise is not free. It is a draw on the writer's attention budget that should be going to scene-level questions.

## Pass 3: line-level

Now, finally, the prose.

Read aloud. Read on paper. Read on a different device than the one you wrote on. The change of medium is the trick that lets you see what you've been re-reading without seeing.

Catch what the next lesson's checklist will catch — passive voice, repeated words, dialogue tag overuse, sentence rhythm, awkward grammar, any line the ear flinches at.

Cut more than you think (callback to [Module 4, Lesson 4](/learn/lesson/world-description-pace) — *the painful part of writing is the cutting*). Most adverbs are stronger when cut. Most adjective-pairs should be one adjective. Most descriptions are one sentence too long.

Pass 3 is the polish. It is the smallest of the three passes by what it changes per page; it is the largest by what it requires per minute. Read slowly. Let the ear catch what the eye doesn't.

## Time between passes

Let the draft cool.

Two weeks is the floor. Six is better.

You cannot revise a draft you can still recite. The mind reads what it expected to write, not what is actually on the page. Time off restores the ability to read what's there.

In the cooling period, write something else. Notes for the next book. A short story. Anything that re-engages writer-mode and lets editor-mode rest. The same principle applies between Pass 2 and Pass 3 — a week off, even just five days, sharpens the line-level pass.

## Tools for re-seeing

A few mechanical tricks that catch what re-reading on the same screen doesn't:

**Print.** Reading on paper catches different problems than reading on screen.

**Different device.** A tablet, a phone, a friend's laptop — anything that breaks the visual pattern of the original document.

**Reading aloud.** The single most reliable way to catch awkward sentences. If your tongue trips on a sentence, the reader's brain trips on it too.

**Different room.** Sounds trivial; works.

These are not gimmicks. They exploit the same thing: a manuscript you have stopped seeing because you've been seeing it the same way for months.

## What this gives you

Three sequential passes — structural, scene, line — in that order, with cooling time between them.

The discipline to *not* fix sentences before you fix structure.

A specific output for each pass.

The next lesson is the *what to check, specifically* version of all three passes — a working checklist with a one-sentence reason for every item.

## Try it

> **Exercise 5.3.1 — Pass 1 only, on one chapter.** Take a chapter from your draft. On a single sheet of paper, write a one-sentence summary of what *each scene* in that chapter does for the story.
>
> *What does this scene do? — In one sentence.*
>
> If you can write the sentence in plain English, the scene is doing work.
>
> If you cannot, the scene is not yet earning its place. Mark it. *Don't fix it yet* — Pass 2 is where the fixes live. Pass 1 produces a list, not a rewrite.
>
> Doing this for one chapter is a fifteen-minute exercise. Doing it for a whole manuscript is the structural pass.
