# The first chapter and the hook

A browsing reader gives you a page. Often less. They read the first paragraph standing in a shop or squinting at a sample, and they decide.

That is not a reason to panic. It is a reason to know exactly what page one is for, because most failed first chapters fail the same way: not badly written, just doing the wrong jobs.

![A theater stage with the curtain rising under the lights](/images/course/photos/stage.jpg "Page one is the curtain going up on the promise your book will spend four hundred pages keeping.")

## The three jobs of page one

Page one has three jobs, and only three.

**Establish the voice.** The reader is not yet invested in your plot; they cannot be, they just got here. What they can fall for instantly is a voice, a way of seeing. The first page is a promise about what the next four hundred will *sound* like.

**Make the promise.** You built your premise as a promise to the reader back in [Module 1](/learn/lesson/foundation-premise-promise). Page one is where that promise gets made in public. A reader opening a thriller should feel a wire tightening. A reader opening a comedy of manners should smirk by paragraph three. Genre, tone, stakes: the opening signs the contract.

**Disturb the normal.** Something on page one should already be off. Not necessarily an explosion. A crack. The day this story starts is different from the day before, and the reader should feel the difference even before they can name it.

Notice what is not on the list: introducing every character, explaining the world, justifying the backstory. Those are jobs for the whole first act, not the first page.

## What page one must not do

The classic openings that kill a book in one paragraph:

- **Weather.** A sky doing things while nobody wants anything.
- **Waking up.** An alarm clock, a yawn, a shower. The character starts the day; the story does not start at all.
- **The mirror.** The protagonist studies their reflection so the author can smuggle in a description. Readers see the trick every time.
- **The backstory dump.** Three pages of history before anything happens now. The reader cannot care about a past when they have not met a present.

None of these are forbidden by law. All of them are almost always a writer warming up, and warming up belongs in the draft, not the book. Which brings us to the single most useful cut in revision: your real opening is often on page three, after the throat-clearing ends.

## The hook is a question, not an explosion

Beginners hear *hook* and reach for gunfire. A body on page one, a bomb, a chase. Sometimes that works. Usually it does not, because spectacle without context is noise: the reader does not yet care who is being shot at.

A hook is smaller and sharper than that. It is a question planted in the reader's head that the reader can only answer by reading on. *Why is she burning those letters? Why does everyone in this village lie about the church? Why does he count his steps?* The question can be quiet. It has to be specific, and it has to be open.

This is why *disturb the normal* is a page-one job. The disturbance is the question. An ordinary morning raises nothing. An ordinary morning where the coffee is made for two and one cup goes untouched raises everything.

- Two pages establishing the heroine's childhood so her later choices make sense
-* A man methodically deletes every photo on his phone except one
X: A hook is a specific open question planted in the reader's head, not spectacle or setup. The meteor is noise without someone to care about, and the backstory answers questions nobody has asked yet.
:::

## Start as late as you can

Every scene, and especially the first, should begin at the last possible moment that still makes sense. Not the drive to the meeting: the meeting. Not the morning of the interview: the moment the interviewer says something wrong.

:::example side-by-side | The same opening, early and late
[Starting early]
Dana's alarm went off at six. She showered, made coffee, and rehearsed her lines on the train, watching the city slide past. At the office she waited twenty minutes outside the glass conference room, smoothing her skirt, until finally her manager waved her in.
[Starting late]
"Before you sit down," her manager said, "you should know the promotion went to Kessler." Dana sat down anyway.
:::

The early version is not badly written. It is just spent on the wrong things: sixty words of runway the reader will forgive but never remember. The late version opens inside the disturbance, and *Dana sat down anyway* is already character.

Cut toward the moment the normal breaks. Trust the reader to infer the train.

## The prologue problem

Here is the honest version: most prologues are chapter-one avoidance. The writer senses their real first chapter is quiet, so they bolt on a battle from three hundred years earlier, or a nameless victim's final minutes, to fake momentum. Readers know. Many skip prologues on principle, which tells you what decades of this have taught them.

The test for the rare prologue that earns its place: it must do a job chapter one *cannot* do, in a POV or time the main narrative can never visit, and the book must be measurably weaker without it. If the information could open chapter one, or wait until chapter ten, it should. When in doubt, cut the prologue and see if anyone, including you, misses it.

## First lines that work, and why

Look at what three famous public-domain first lines actually do.

*"It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife."* Austen's line establishes voice (ironic, worldly, amused) and promises the whole book: marriage as economics, narrated by someone who sees through it. The plot has not started. The contract is already signed.

*"It was the best of times, it was the worst of times."* Dickens promises scale and contradiction: a story where every fortune has its shadow, told by a narrator big enough to hold a whole era. The rhythm itself is the hook; you lean in to hear where the sentence lands.

*"Call me Ishmael."* Three words. A voice talking directly to you, casual and strange at once, and a question planted immediately: *call* me Ishmael, so is that his name or not? Melville hooks you with a narrator you already want to hear more from.

Three different lines, one common trade: each makes a promise about voice and asks a question. None of them describe weather.

:::check
Q: What do these three famous first lines have in common, according to this lesson?
- Each opens with dramatic physical action to seize attention
-* Each establishes a voice and plants a promise or question before the plot begins
- Each introduces the protagonist by name and appearance immediately
X: None of the three opens with action, and only one contains a name at all. They work because voice plus an open question is the real hook; plot can wait a page, the contract cannot.
:::

## Draft it imperfect and move

Last thing, and it matters most for the draft you are about to write. Your first chapter will be rewritten after the book is finished. Not polished: rewritten. You cannot fully make the opening promise until you know exactly what the book delivers, and you will not know that until the last chapter exists.

So do not spend six weeks polishing page one before drafting page two. That is the most common graveyard in this whole course. Write a placeholder opening that is roughly aimed, plant your best guess at the question, and go. The next lesson is about keeping that forward motion for an entire draft.

## Try it

:::exercise
**The page-one audit.** Write the first 250 words of your novel, or take the 250 you already have. Then answer, in one sentence each:

- What question does this opening plant in the reader's head?
- What does the voice promise about the next four hundred pages?
- What is disturbed about the normal here?
- Could this opening start later? Find the latest moment in these 250 words where the story could begin, and rewrite the opening to start there.

If you cannot answer the first question, you do not yet have a hook. You have a beginning. Revise once until the question exists.
:::

Take this with you.

:::takeaway
- Page one has three jobs: establish the voice, make the premise's promise, disturb the normal.
- Weather, waking up, mirrors, and backstory dumps are warm-up, and warm-up belongs in the draft, not the book.
- A hook is a specific open question planted in the reader's head; an explosion without context is just noise.
- Start at the last possible moment: the promotion, not the coffee. Most prologues are chapter-one avoidance; cut unless the prologue does a job chapter one cannot.
- Your first chapter gets rewritten after the draft is done, so write it imperfect and keep moving.
:::

## Further reading

All public domain.

- Jane Austen, *Pride and Prejudice*. Read the first chapter only, twice: once for the famous opening line's promise, once to watch how fast the dialogue takes over and how little setup Austen thinks you need. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/1342](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1342))
- Charles Dickens, *A Tale of Two Cities*. The opening paragraph is the boldest contract in the language: pure voice, zero scene, and it works. Study it as the extreme case of promise before plot. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/98](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/98))
- Herman Melville, *Moby-Dick*. Three words of first line, then a narrator explaining why he goes to sea whenever life sours. Watch how long Melville sustains the hook on voice alone. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/2701](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/2701))
