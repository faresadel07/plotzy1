# Why people read fiction

Ask people why they read novels and most will say "to escape."

That's true sometimes. It's incomplete every time.

People who only want to escape go to sleep. They scroll their phone. They drink. They don't sit silently in a chair for three hours, holding still, working through someone else's problem.

Whatever fiction does, it's stronger than escape.

## The empathy machine

You have never lost a child. You read a novel about a woman who has lost a child. You finish it on a train and you can't speak for half an hour.

What just happened to you?

You ran her loss on your own simulator. The brain handed you the equipment of grief — the heaviness of it, the small involuntary glance at empty rooms — and let you experience them in safety. By the time you closed the book, you had practised an emotion you have never lived through.

This is what fiction does that an argument cannot. An argument tells you about another person's life. Fiction puts you inside it. The first delivers data. The second delivers rehearsal.

For the writer, this matters at the line level.

A character in pain, on the page, is not enough. The reader has to be set up to *feel* the pain, not be told about it. You won't earn the train-ride silence with "she was devastated." You earn it with what she does with her hands while she packs the things she should give away. (We'll do this kind of work properly in Module 4. For now: notice that it's the work.)

Readers don't read for description. They read to feel things they wouldn't otherwise feel. That's not a soft motive. That's why your reader is willing to sit with you for nine hours.

## The social simulator

Here is a useful way to think about a novel: it is a low-stakes laboratory for being someone else.

How does a 19th-century Russian aristocrat handle being snubbed at a ball? How does a man who has spent his life at sea decide whether to kill the only thing he has ever obsessed about? How does a young woman who has never had a sister learn to grieve one?

You can run all three of those experiments before bed. You don't have to be Russian. You don't have to be at sea. You don't have to lose anyone.

A reader will tolerate slow patches in a story if those patches are teaching them something about *how a person works*. They will not tolerate slow patches that teach them nothing.

This is one of the most useful tests for whether a scene is earning its place. After this scene, has the reader learned something they didn't know about being a person? If yes, the scene works even if the prose is plain. If no, no amount of beautiful prose will save it.

## Why genres exist

Each genre is a contract about which hunger the reader brought.

A **mystery** reader walked in with the suspicion that the world is illegible — that bad things happen for no reason. They want, by the last page, the world to make sense. The contract is *something will be solved*.

A **romance** reader walked in with the suspicion that two people can meet and recognise each other accurately. They want, by the last page, evidence that this can happen. The contract is *recognition is possible*.

A **horror** reader walked in with a fear they keep just under the surface in daily life. They want, by the last page, to have looked at it head-on and survived. The contract is *the worst thing can be looked at*.

A **tragedy** reader walked in carrying a loss they don't know how to grieve. They want, by the last page, confirmation that some losses are real and unfixable. The contract is *some grief is allowed*.

When you know which contract your reader signed, you know what they will and won't forgive you for. A mystery without resolution betrays the contract. A romance with no recognition does too. The contracts aren't formulas — within them you have enormous freedom — but breaking them costs you the reader you'd already won.

## The reader is rooting for you

In Lesson 1 we said the reader will look for pattern in whatever you give them.

Here's the practical version of that idea: the reader is *trying to find* meaning in your sentences. They are not a hostile audience. They opened your book because they wanted it to work. Every line they read, they are quietly hoping the next line will be the one that makes the previous line worth it.

This changes how you write.

Most beginners write as if they're convincing a reluctant reader. They over-explain. They re-state the emotion. They protect against being misunderstood. The result is prose that condescends to a reader who was already on its side.

Your reader showed up. They're holding the book. They want it to work.

Trust them. Write as if you're being read by someone who's already in.

## What this gives you

A clearer picture of what your reader actually wants from your novel: not events, not even prose, but rehearsal in a life they can't otherwise live.

A test for any scene: does it teach the reader something about how a person works?

A reminder for hard drafting days: your reader is on your side before you've earned them. Don't write down to them. Write up to them.

## Try it

> **Exercise 2.1 — The book that argued.** Pick a book that changed your view on something — a relationship, a place, a kind of person, an idea. In 250 words, describe what the book did that an argument couldn't.
>
> Be specific. What was the moment it landed? What did you understand differently after that moment than you'd understood before?
>
> If you can't think of a book that has done this, that's information too — about what you've been reading and what you might want to read next.

## Further reading

All public domain. Free at Project Gutenberg.

- Leo Tolstoy, *Anna Karenina*. The ball scene where Kitty is overlooked runs about forty pages of what looks, on the surface, like a young woman watching another young woman dance. It is the best demonstration in literature of what an *internal* event feels like. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/1399](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1399))
- Herman Melville, *Moby-Dick*. Read it for the question of what an obsession costs the people around it. The whale is the surface; Ahab is the experiment. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/2701](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/2701))
- George Eliot, *Middlemarch*. A novel about ordinary marriages and ordinary careers that turns out to be the best social simulator in nineteenth-century English fiction. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/145](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/145))
