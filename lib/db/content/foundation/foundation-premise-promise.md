# The premise and the promise

Here is an idea: a haunted house.

Here is a premise: a broke, grieving mother moves her kids into a cheap old house, and the house starts leaving her notes in her dead son's handwriting, and she cannot afford to leave.

One of these you can write tomorrow morning. The other you can only stare at.

If you did the work in [Finding your story idea](/learn/lesson/foundation-finding-idea), you have a notebook full of the first kind. Sparks. Images. What-ifs. Good. That's the raw material. This lesson is about the machine that turns one of them into something you can actually draft: a single sentence, and a promise.

## An idea is a place. A premise is a direction.

An idea names a subject. A premise names a *pressure*.

"A haunted house" tells you where the story happens. It doesn't tell you whose story it is, what they're trying to do, what's stopping them, or why we should care if they fail. You can add atmosphere to an idea forever and never start a story, because atmosphere doesn't force anything to happen next.

A premise forces things. Someone specific wants something specific, something specific resists, and losing would cost something the reader can feel. Write that in one sentence and you've built a slope. Events roll down it.

The formula is not glamorous, and it works:

**Character + want + obstacle + stakes.**

:::example side-by-side | Same spark, before and after the machine.
[Idea]
A small town where everyone lies.
[Premise]
A young sheriff who cannot tell a lie must find her father's killer in a town where everyone lies to her face, before the killer becomes the next mayor and buries the case for good.
:::

Notice what the premise version did. It picked a person (and gave her a flaw wired directly into the problem). It named a want with a finish line. It made the obstacle *specific to her*: a truth-teller drowning in liars. And it put a clock and a cost on failure.

You met these parts in miniature already, and you'll dissect them properly in [the 3 ingredients every story needs](/learn/lesson/foundation-three-ingredients). The premise sentence is where they first meet each other on paper.

## Three tests before you commit

Not every sentence that fits the formula is worth a year of your life. Run these three tests.

**Can you see the shape of an ending?** Not the ending itself. The shape. The sheriff finds the killer or she doesn't; either way you can feel where the road goes. If your premise could end absolutely anywhere, it isn't a slope yet, it's still a field.

**Is the conflict specific?** "A detective faces evil" is a horoscope. "A truth-teller must out-lie a town of liars" is a story problem, because the obstacle presses on exactly who this character is. The best premises have the obstacle and the character locked together so tightly you couldn't swap in a different protagonist without breaking the sentence.

**Would a stranger ask "what happens next?"** Say your sentence out loud to someone who owes you nothing. If they ask a question, any question, you have a premise. If they say "nice" and change the subject, you have an idea wearing a premise's clothes. This test is brutal and it is the cheapest feedback you will ever get.

:::check
Q: "A story about a lonely lighthouse keeper" fails as a premise mainly because it lacks what?
- A protagonist the reader can care about
-* A want, an obstacle, and stakes that force events to happen
- An original setting, since lighthouses are overused
X: It has a protagonist and a setting. What's missing is the pressure: nothing in the sentence forces a next event. Loneliness is a condition, not a conflict, until the keeper wants something and something resists.
:::

## The promise: what your first pages sign the reader up for

Now the second half of this lesson, and the half almost nobody teaches beginners.

The moment a stranger reads your opening pages, your book makes a promise. Not in a mission statement. In everything: the genre signals, the tone of the sentences, the kind of problem on page one, the question the opening plants. A body in chapter one promises a solution. A meet-cute promises a relationship worth rooting for. Dread on page one promises that the dread will pay off. Jokes on page one promise more jokes.

The reader signs this contract without noticing, and then holds you to it ruthlessly.

Here's the part worth tattooing somewhere. Readers will forgive an enormous amount: slow chapters, clumsy sentences, a subplot that wanders. What they will not forgive is a broken promise. The thriller that turns into a meditation on grief at the midpoint. The comedy that stops being funny. The mystery that shrugs. When a reader says "it got slow," go look at the page where they stopped. Nine times out of ten the book wasn't slow there. It had drifted from the question it opened with, and the reader, no longer being paid in the currency they signed up for, quietly left.

:::quote
Readers don't quit slow books. They quit broken promises.
:::

And the promise reaches further than tone and genre. The central question your premise poses implies who your character must become to answer it. A sheriff who cannot lie, in a town of liars, is being promised (and so is your reader) a collision between her honesty and her goal. The premise doesn't just start the plot. It sketches the arc.

:::check
Q: A reader abandons a novel at the 40 percent mark and says "it just got slow." What's the most likely structural cause?
- The chapters were too long for modern attention spans
- The prose style needed more action verbs
-* The book drifted from the question and tone its opening pages promised
X: "Slow" is usually the reader's word for "no longer paying me in the currency I signed up for." Pacing complaints most often mark the page where the book stopped answering to its own opening promise.
:::

## The compass

One more job for your sentence, and it may be the most valuable: it is your compass while drafting.

Every writer gets lost in the middle of a draft. Every one. You'll be forty thousand words in, three subplots deep, and unable to remember what the book is about. When that happens, do not reread your manuscript. Reread your sentence.

The sentence tells you what belongs. That gorgeous chapter where the sheriff's deputy visits his mother? Does it press on *truth-teller versus town of liars*? If yes, keep it. If no, it's a beautiful stranger in your house. The premise is the cheapest editorial tool you own: one line, written before the mess existed, that remembers what you meant.

Some writers tape it above the desk. Do that literally. You'll roll your eyes exactly once.

:::checklist
- One sentence: character, want, obstacle, stakes
- I can see the shape of an ending
- The obstacle is specific to this character
- A stranger asked "what happens next?"
- It's taped where I can see it
:::

## Try it

:::exercise
**Build the sentence, then test the promise.** Take your strongest idea from the last lesson (or grab a fresh one). Write it as one premise sentence: a specific character, a concrete want, an obstacle locked to who that character is, and a cost for failure.

Then write three more versions of the same premise, changing exactly one element each time: swap the character, then the obstacle, then the stakes. Pick the version that makes *you* ask what happens next.

Finally, under the winning sentence, write two lines describing the promise its opening pages would make: the genre and tone the reader signs up for, and the central question they'll expect answered by the last page. Together, that's your contract. About 150 words total.
:::

The premise gives you the sentence. The next lesson, [the 3 ingredients every story needs](/learn/lesson/foundation-three-ingredients), takes that sentence apart and shows you how each piece actually works.

:::takeaway
- An idea names a subject; a premise names a pressure: character, want, obstacle, stakes in one sentence.
- Test a premise three ways: you can see an ending's shape, the conflict is specific to this character, and a stranger asks "what happens next?"
- Your opening pages make a promise of genre, tone, and central question, and the reader holds you to it.
- Readers don't quit slow books; they quit books that broke their promise.
- When you're lost mid-draft, don't reread the manuscript. Reread your sentence.
:::

## Further reading

All public domain. Free at Project Gutenberg.

- H. G. Wells, *The War of the Worlds*. A premise so clean you can say it in six words: Martians invade, one man must survive. Watch how the opening chapter makes its promise of dread and scale, then keeps it to the last page. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/36](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/36))
- Charles Dickens, *A Christmas Carol*. Premise as compass at its purest: a miser is shown his past, present, and future in one night, and must change or die unmourned. Every scene answers to that sentence. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/46](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/46))
- Bram Stoker, *Dracula*. The first four chapters are a masterclass in promise-making: the dread planted in Harker's journal is a debt the entire novel then pays. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/345](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/345))
