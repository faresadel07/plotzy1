# Supporting cast

Every supporting character earns their page time or steals it.

The cull is brutal because most beginners' drafts are full of characters who *would* be doing work if the writer asked them to — but the writer hasn't.

This lesson teaches you to ask.

## Functional vs. dimensional

A **functional** supporting character does a single job in the story. The mentor who delivers the tool. The rival who applies pressure. The confidant who lets the protagonist say their thoughts aloud.

A **dimensional** supporting character does the function plus has *their own visible life*. The mentor who is also tired of being asked. The rival who is genuinely surprised when they win. The confidant who is, secretly, in love with someone the protagonist hasn't noticed.

Most supporting characters in a novel should be functional. A book with twelve dimensional characters reads like an ensemble drama — sometimes the right choice, often a mess. The reader's working memory is finite. Every dimension you add to a side character is dimension the reader has to track.

The skill is knowing which characters earn the second dimension and which are overstaying their welcome.

## The two-trait rule

A supporting character with one trait is cardboard.

The brave knight. The stern father. The wise old woman.

A supporting character with two traits is a person.

The brave knight who is also a coward about heights. The stern father who keeps a child's drawing in his coat pocket. The wise old woman who is wrong, sometimes, and likes to be argued with.

Two specifically *unrelated* traits. *Brave and noble* is one trait pretending to be two. *Brave and afraid of heights* is two, because the reader's mind has to hold a small contradiction.

The rule isn't a recipe. It's a minimum. It is the line between cardboard and person, and it's a line many drafts fall on the wrong side of without the writer noticing.

## The classical roles, used carefully

The roles below are tools, not slots. Naming them is not a substitute for writing the character.

**The mentor.** Gives the protagonist a tool, knowledge, or permission to act. Worked best when the mentor is also a person with their own losses. Worked worst when the mentor is a wisdom-dispenser without a body.

**The foil.** Defined by contrast with the protagonist. Shows the reader who the protagonist is by being *almost* like them but not quite. Mr. Bennet is a foil to Elizabeth — same intellect, different ethical compass — and the contrast is one of the things that makes Elizabeth's choices feel earned. A good foil is rarely a villain. The point is comparison, not opposition.

**The confidant.** Lets the protagonist speak their thoughts aloud without it sounding like internal monologue. Worked best when the confidant *pushes back* — confidants who only listen are, again, function-shaped. The best confidants are wrong half the time.

**The rival.** Wants something the protagonist wants. Different from the antagonist (whose want directly opposes the protagonist's); the rival is in parallel pursuit. A rival is often more useful than an antagonist for stories about ambition. When the rival becomes a friend, you have a different kind of subplot than the rival who never softens.

These roles are starting places. The character *exceeds* the role or the character is filler.

## When to cut, when to merge

The cut question: *what would the story lose if I removed this character?*

If the answer is "nothing" — cut.

If the answer is "another character would have to do their job" — consider merging.

Two minor characters with overlapping functions are almost always one minor character. A chapter with eleven named characters performs worse than the same chapter with seven. The reader is doing more work and getting less out of the room.

When you merge, the character that emerges is usually richer than either of the originals. Combine the mentor and the rival into one figure and you get a much more interesting kind of help — guidance from someone who has their own stake in the outcome. Combine the confidant and the mother and you get a confidant whose advice carries weight she didn't grant herself.

Merging is not a defeat. It's a concentration.

## Cardboard demotes the protagonist

This is the part of the lesson that surprises most writers.

A real protagonist (with the wound and want from [Lesson 1](/learn/lesson/characters-protagonist-wound)) surrounded by cardboard *demotes* the protagonist. The reader feels the imbalance. If everyone else in the room is a function, the protagonist starts to feel function-shaped too.

The same applies to the antagonist (L3). A real antagonist with cardboard henchmen is a real antagonist who looks like they are acting alone in a populated world.

The fix is not to make every supporting character dimensional. The fix is to make sure that *whichever* supporting characters share the page with your protagonist for any length of time have the second trait. The barista who appears once doesn't need it. The aunt who appears in three scenes does.

## What this gives you

A diagnostic for every secondary character in your draft.

A way to merge or cut without losing structural function.

A practical link to [Module 2's structure lesson](/learn/lesson/architecture-choosing-structure): supporting cast is in service of the structure you chose. Characters whose function in the structure is unclear are usually structural patches in disguise — the writer needed someone to deliver information at the midpoint and invented a character to do it. Often the better fix is to have an existing character carry the information, not to add a new face.

## Try it

> **Exercise 3.4.1 — Cull the cast.** List every supporting character in your premise (M1, Exercise 3.2). For each, answer two questions.
>
> 1. What is their *function* — the single job they do for the story?
> 2. What is their *second* trait — the one unrelated to their function that turns them from cardboard into a person?
>
> If you cannot answer (1), the character is not yet doing work. Cut them, or give them a job.
>
> If you cannot answer (2), the character is cardboard. Add a second trait, or accept that they're a function-only role and write them sparely (one line, one moment).
>
> If two characters share the same answer to (1), consider merging them. The character that comes out of the merger will almost always be more interesting than either of the originals.

## Further reading

All public domain. Free at Project Gutenberg.

- *Pride and Prejudice* (Austen, 1813). Mrs. Bennet (function: pressure on Elizabeth) and Mr. Bennet (function: foil + confidant). Two-trait rule in action: Mr. Bennet is witty *and* derelict; Mrs. Bennet is anxious *and* shrewd in marriage-economics. Both characters earn their page time. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/1342](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1342))
- *The Pickwick Papers* (Dickens, 1836–37). Sam Weller, the savvy servant. Functional role with two traits (loyal *and* cynical) that make him outlive most of the cast in the reader's memory. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/580](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/580))
- *Macbeth* (Shakespeare, ~1606). Banquo, the moral mirror. He is on stage for less time than most readers remember, and what he does is mostly to stand near Macbeth and hold a different kind of life. The contrast is the work. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/2264](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/2264))
