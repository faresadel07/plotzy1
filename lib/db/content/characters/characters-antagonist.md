# The antagonist (and why they need to be human)

Default to humanising your antagonist.

Reach for genuine evil only when you can earn it.

That's the whole lesson. The rest is how to do both.

## Antagonist ≠ villain

An antagonist *opposes*. A villain *is evil*. Most antagonists are not villains.

Most are people whose want collides with the protagonist's. The cousin who inherits what should have been theirs. The colleague who is promoted instead. The childhood friend who married the person you were supposed to marry.

Mr. Wickham, in *Pride and Prejudice*, is an antagonist. Not a villain. He is charming, self-interested, and not especially cruel. He works as Elizabeth's antagonist because his want — a comfortable life — is in direct contradiction with hers — to be seen accurately. His method (deception) is a thing she has to learn to read. By the end of the novel, having read him, she is more capable than she was. The antagonist made the protagonist.

A villain is a moral problem. An antagonist is a structural problem with the protagonist's path. They need different tools. The instinct to write a villain when an antagonist would do — to make the opposition *evil* rather than *opposed* — flattens the conflict. Now there is only one side to the argument.

## Mirror the protagonist's framework

Whatever you did for your protagonist in [Lesson 1](/learn/lesson/characters-protagonist-wound), do for your antagonist.

**Wound.** What specific past event made them this person? The cousin who inherited what should have been theirs. The system that promoted someone less qualified. The friend who chose someone else.

**Want.** What can they name in a sentence? *I want her to fail. I want my position back. I want to be the one who tells the truth about what happened.*

**Need.** Almost certainly different from their want. The antagonist usually doesn't see their need any more clearly than the protagonist sees theirs.

The closer your antagonist's wound and want come to *paralleling* your protagonist's, the more dangerous they become. The strongest antagonists are people who could have been the protagonist of a different book — and who, in this book, want what the protagonist wants for reasons they can name out loud.

When you've done this work properly, you have two characters with parallel architecture moving toward each other. Their collision *is* the plot.

## They think they're the hero

The line gets quoted to death because it's true. Most people in conflict believe themselves justified. The antagonist who can be made to articulate that justification — out loud, in their own voice, without authorial irony — is the antagonist who can carry a novel.

This is not a moral claim. It's an observational one. Even a genuinely cruel person believes their cruelty is reasonable in context. The novelist's job is to find the context. Not to endorse it.

The exercise at the end of this lesson is the practical version. Write a paragraph from your antagonist's point of view, sympathetic to themselves, where they explain why they are right.

If you can't, your antagonist isn't ready. They are not a person yet. They are a function.

If you can — read the paragraph once and ask whether your *protagonist's* want, in light of what the antagonist just said, still feels worth a novel. If yes, your conflict is real. If no, that's information about which of the two characters needs more work.

## When evil is the right choice

Genuine evil — the antagonist whose motives don't reduce to a comprehensible want — exists in literature.

Iago is the canonical example. Across *Othello* he gives several speeches attempting to explain his hatred, and none of them quite hold together. He says he was passed over for promotion. He says Othello slept with his wife (Iago has no real reason to believe this). He says he envies Othello's nobility. None of these explanations are *false*; none of them is *enough*. The play is aware of this and uses it. Iago is not a man whose want we are meant to understand. He is a force with a face.

Genuine evil works as a choice when:

- The story is mythic in scale and the antagonist is a *force* the protagonist must survive.
- The point of the story is the protagonist's confrontation with something inexplicable. Some kinds of grief and some kinds of fear cannot be reasoned with on the page.
- You can hold the evil at the right distance — close enough that the reader feels it, far enough that the reader doesn't try to diagnose it.

Genuine evil fails as a choice when:

- It's a shortcut. *He's evil because he's evil* is the antagonist version of *she's strong because she's strong*. The reader sees through both immediately.
- The story is intimate in scale. Domestic novels and quiet family dramas almost always need an antagonist with a comprehensible wound. Evil in a small room reads as melodrama.
- It's used to avoid character work. When a writer hasn't built the antagonist's wound, declaring them *evil* is the costume that hides the absence.

Default to humanising. Reach for evil only when the story's scale, scope, and atmosphere can hold it.

## The system as antagonist

Sometimes the antagonist isn't a person. Poverty. War. An illness. An institution.

System-as-antagonist works because it gives the protagonist an obstacle that cannot be reasoned with. The kind of obstacle that forces the deepest internal change (callback to the conflict ingredient in [Module 1](/learn/lesson/foundation-three-ingredients)).

The risk is that a system can be too abstract to fight. *Poverty* is not a character. *Poverty* doesn't enter the room and refuse to leave. *Poverty* doesn't sweat or snore or laugh at the wrong moment.

Most great novels with system antagonists *also* have a human face for the system somewhere. A specific officer. A specific landlord. A specific bureaucrat across a desk. The system carries the weight; the person makes it concrete enough to confront.

The reader needs both. The system, to feel the scale. The person, to feel the encounter.

## What this gives you

A second character with the same architecture as your protagonist. Wound, want, need.

A test: can you write a paragraph from your antagonist's point of view in which they sound *right*?

A clearer picture of what the conflict ingredient from M1 L4 looks like at full strength. A specific obstacle is much sharper when the obstacle has wants of its own.

## Try it

> **Exercise 3.3.1 — The antagonist's case.** Take your antagonist. Write a single paragraph from their point of view, sympathetic to themselves, in which they explain why they are *right*.
>
> No irony. No authorial commentary. No adjectives that tell the reader what to think. Just the antagonist talking, in their own voice, making their case.
>
> If you cannot write the paragraph, your antagonist isn't ready. You have a function, not a person.
>
> If you can — read it once and ask: in light of what the antagonist just said, does my protagonist's want still feel worth a novel? If yes, the conflict is in good shape. If no, that's information about which of the two characters needs more work.

## Further reading

All public domain. Free at Project Gutenberg.

- *Othello* (Shakespeare, ~1603). Iago, for genuine-evil-done-well. Pay attention to the soliloquies — the explanations that don't quite cohere are the point. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/1531](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1531))
- *Pride and Prejudice* (Austen, 1813). Mr. Wickham, for the charming antagonist with his own logic. Wickham is given a long speech early in the book in which he explains himself; how Elizabeth (and the reader) hear it changes between first reading and second. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/1342](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1342))
- *Les Misérables* (Hugo, 1862). Inspector Javert, for the principled antagonist — a man whose moral framework forces him to oppose someone the reader is rooting for. The chapters in which Javert makes his final decision are the cleanest study of an antagonist as a person in nineteenth-century fiction. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/135](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/135))
- *Frankenstein* (Shelley, 1818). For the antagonist-question itself: in this book, who is whose antagonist? Reading it once with Victor as protagonist and again with the creature as protagonist is the fastest demonstration of how the framework reframes a story. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/84](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/84))
