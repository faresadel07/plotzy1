# Point of view: choosing the camera

Every sentence in your book passes through a camera. Point of view is where you bolt that camera down.

It is the one craft decision that touches literally every line. Word choice, what the reader knows, what the reader feels, which scenes are even possible: all of it is downstream of POV. Get it right and the book feels inevitable. Get it wrong and no amount of sentence polish will save it, because the sentences themselves are the problem.

Most craft decisions can be revised. This one can only be rewritten. That is why it belongs at the end of the character module, before you draft a word.

## The options, with honest tradeoffs

There are three camera positions that matter. Each buys you something and costs you something. Anyone who tells you one is simply better is selling their own preference.

**First person.** *I walked into the room.* The camera is inside one skull, and the narration itself is characterization. Every observation, every joke, every blind spot belongs to the narrator. This is the intimacy option: the reader spends four hundred pages in one voice, and if that voice is good, the book is good.

The cost: you are locked to one head. Your narrator must be present for every scene that matters, or hear about it secondhand, which is weaker. If your plot needs the reader to see the antagonist alone in a room, first person cannot give it to you. And the voice must carry the whole book. A flat first-person narrator is a flat book, with nowhere to hide.

**Third person limited.** *She walked into the room.* The camera sits on one character's shoulder per scene. The reader gets that character's thoughts and perceptions and nothing else. This is the modern default, and it is the default for a reason: it keeps most of first person's intimacy while letting you switch shoulders at scene or chapter breaks. Multiple viewpoint characters, one at a time, cleanly.

The cost: discipline. Within a scene you know only what your viewpoint character knows. If she cannot see it, the reader cannot see it. Break that rule and you are head-hopping, which we will get to.

**Omniscient.** The camera belongs to a narrator who knows everything, can enter any head, can pull back to comment on the whole town or the whole century. Dickens and Austen wrote this way, and the scope is glorious.

The cost: it is the hardest mode to do well, because the narrator must be a *presence*, a consistent voice with opinions and a vantage point, not a camera that drifts wherever is convenient. Written without that controlling voice, omniscient collapses into accidental head-hopping, and readers trained on a century of limited narration read it as error, not style.

## Head-hopping, demonstrated

Head-hopping is jumping between characters' inner lives mid-scene without a controlling omniscient voice. It is the classic beginner POV mistake, and it is worth seeing side by side.

:::example side-by-side | The same moment, hopped and clean
[Head-hopped]
Sara slid the letter across the desk, hoping he would not notice her hands shaking. Marcus took it, wondering why she looked so pale, and decided not to ask. She watched him read, terrified. He felt a knot form in his stomach at the second paragraph.
[Clean third limited (Sara's head)]
Sara slid the letter across the desk and pressed her hands flat against her thighs to stop them shaking. Marcus took it. He read the first paragraph with no expression at all. At the second, something crossed his face, there and gone, and he set the letter down without looking at her.
:::

In the hopped version, the reader is told what both characters feel, and the scene has no tension, because there is nothing left to wonder about. In the clean version the reader is inside Sara, reading Marcus the way Sara has to read him: from the outside, through expression and gesture. The uncertainty *is* the scene.

That is the deeper point. POV discipline is not a rule for its own sake. Limiting the camera creates the gap between what your character knows and what they need to know, and that gap is where tension lives. Every head you enter is a mystery you delete.

## Narrative distance is a dial, not a switch

Even inside third limited, you have a dial. At one end, the camera pulls back: *She entered the courthouse at nine.* Reportorial, cool, fast. At the other end, the camera sinks all the way into the character's skull and the narration takes on her diction: *Nine a.m. and already the place smelled like fear and floor polish.* That second register, third person wearing the character's voice, is close third, and it is most of what makes modern third limited feel intimate.

Skilled writers slide this dial constantly. Pull back for transitions and time skips. Push in for the moments that hurt. The mistake is not moving the dial; the mistake is jerking it randomly, or sitting at maximum closeness for four hundred pages until the reader goes numb.

Your character's voice work from earlier in this module ([dialogue](/learn/lesson/characters-dialogue), the wound) pays off here. Close third is your character's voice leaking into the narration. If you built the voice, the dial is already wired.

## A word on tense

Past tense is the default. It is invisible, the way *said* is invisible, and it handles time flexibly: flashback, summary, and scene all move naturally.

Present tense is a flavor choice. It trades flexibility for immediacy, a sense that events are unspooling now and nobody, including the narrator, knows how they end. It suits some books. It draws attention to itself in others. If you are unsure, write past. Nobody has ever put down a book for being in past tense.

:::check
Q: In a close third limited scene, your viewpoint character's estranged father says he is proud of her. Which line breaks POV?
- Her throat tightened, and she hated that it did
- He said it flatly, the way he said everything, and she could not tell if he meant it
-* He meant it, though she would never believe him
X: The viewpoint character cannot know what her father means; only he knows that. The first option is her own reaction and the second is her reading him from outside, both of which stay inside her head.
:::

## Choose it, then lock it

Here is the instruction this whole lesson exists to deliver. Before you draft, choose your POV, your viewpoint characters, and your tense. Write them on a card. Then lock the card.

Because switching POV mid-draft is not an edit. It is a rewrite. Every sentence carries the fingerprints of its camera: what got noticed, what got named, which thoughts made the page. Converting a first-person book to third means rebuilding every paragraph, not finding and replacing pronouns. Writers who switch at chapter twelve almost never finish, because the first eleven chapters are now a debt.

:::quote
Doubt your outline, doubt your characters, doubt your title. Do not doubt your camera at forty thousand words.
:::

If you genuinely cannot choose, run the test in the exercise below. Then commit. A decent POV executed with total consistency beats the perfect POV you keep re-litigating.

## Try it

:::exercise
**The camera test.** Take one key scene from your story, the inciting incident or another moment you know well, and write its opening 200 words three times:

- First person, in your protagonist's voice.
- Close third limited, camera on your protagonist's shoulder.
- Distant third or omniscient, camera pulled back.

Then answer in two or three sentences: which version created tension by *withholding* something, and which version told the reader too much? Pick the POV you are locking for the draft, and write one line committing to it, including tense.
:::

:::takeaway
- POV is the one decision that touches every sentence; it is rewritten, never revised.
- First person buys intimacy and voice at the cost of one locked head; third limited is the modern default; omniscient buys scope but demands a controlling narrator voice.
- Head-hopping deletes tension: the gap between what your viewpoint character knows and needs to know is where the scene lives.
- Narrative distance is a dial inside third limited: pull back to move fast, push in when it hurts.
- Choose POV, viewpoint characters, and tense before drafting, then lock them.
:::

## Further reading

All public domain.

- Charlotte Bronte, *Jane Eyre*. The reference point for first-person intimacy: the voice addresses you directly, withholds, confesses, and the book's power is inseparable from being locked inside one head for its whole length. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/1260](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1260))
- Henry James, *The Turn of the Screw*. A masterclass in what a strictly limited camera makes possible: the entire horror of the book lives in the gap between what the governess perceives and what is actually there. Limitation as engine. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/209](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/209))
- Jane Austen, *Pride and Prejudice*. Omniscient done with a controlling voice, and the early home of the close-third technique: watch how the narration slides in and out of Elizabeth's judgments without ever losing its own ironic vantage. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/1342](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1342))
