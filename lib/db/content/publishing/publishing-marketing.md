# Marketing your first book

Marketing for first-time authors is mostly waste.

Accept this. The book itself is the best marketing for the *next* book. The first launch is an exercise in three honest tactics, three honest refusals, and a long view — book 3 starts when book 1 ships.

This lesson contains the won't-do list. **The won't-do list matters more than the do list.** That is not a literary flourish; it is the lesson.

## Most first-launch marketing is waste

First-time authors spend money on launch marketing and most of it produces nothing. Cold ads to readers who don't know your name. Blog tours where the blogs have audiences smaller than your newsletter. Paid placement that disappears in the first 48 hours.

This is not pessimism. It is the math. You have not yet built the conditions in which marketing dollars convert. The conditions are: an audience that knows you ([Lesson 3](/learn/lesson/publishing-audience)), a cover that signals genre ([Lesson 2](/learn/lesson/publishing-cover-blurb)), a book that delivers (Modules 1–5). When those three are in place, marketing dollars work. Before they are, marketing is friction without compounding.

The fix is not *more marketing*. The fix is *honest marketing* — the small list of things that actually work for a first book — plus a frame that values consistency over one big launch.

## Three honest tactics

**The newsletter announcement.**

Your list (Lesson 3) is the highest-converting audience you'll ever have. A pre-launch sequence — three to five emails over the weeks before publication — converts at rates that paid advertising cannot match. Without a list, this tactic doesn't exist; with one, it is the foundation of every honest launch.

The sequence: announce the cover, share an excerpt, give the launch date, ask for pre-orders, send on launch day. Each email lands with people who already opted in. The conversion is not extraordinary; the audience pre-sorted itself.

**Asking for reviews — directly, from readers you respect.**

Twenty thoughtful reviews on launch week change the book's discoverability on most retailers. Most of those reviews come from a writer asking specific readers — people who already enjoyed your work, beta readers, newsletter subscribers — for the favour.

Honest, transparent ask. Not a brigade. Not a swap. Just: *I valued your read; would you write a few sentences if you mean them?* Most readers will. The few who say no are not insulted by the ask.

**Genuine presence in your genre's communities.**

Genre readers cluster — there are forums, podcasts, review sites, blogs, occasionally physical conventions. Show up to the ones you care about. Read other writers' books in your genre. Comment thoughtfully on conversations. Be a person.

Years before launch is best; months are workable; the day before launch is too late. *Genuine* is the operative word. The community can tell when someone shows up two weeks before launch with a book to push, and it does not respond well.

## Three tactics that don't work

**Cold social media posts.**

*Hello strangers, please buy my book* posts perform at single-digit click rates and zero-digit conversion rates. The audience for this kind of post is the writer's existing followers, who would have heard about the book anyway. The strangers scroll past.

Posting *about* the book to your existing social audience is fine — you have a relationship with them. Posting *at* strangers is what doesn't work.

**Paid ads without an audience.**

Paid ads work when you have *retargeting data* and *conversion infrastructure* — neither of which a first-time author has. Most first-launch ads are unprofitable; the writer cannot tell because they don't have the data to measure conversion against, and they spend three to ten times the eventual royalty earned to acquire each reader.

Ads are useful at book 3, after you've built the data and the cover designs and the audience that ads can amplify. They are usually unprofitable at book 1.

**The blog tour.**

Twelve guest posts on twelve writing blogs over launch week. Each blog has a tiny audience. The total reach is below the writer's own newsletter. The time spent could have been a week of work on book 2.

Blog tours used to work, in the era when blog audiences were larger and writing blogs were the centre of the conversation. They mostly don't now. The exception: if the blog has a substantial audience in *your specific genre*, the post might be worth the effort. Most blogs do not.

## The thread connecting the three failures

Each of the three tactics that don't work treats *strangers* as the launch audience.

Strangers convert poorly. The honest tactics treat *readers who already know you* as the launch audience. Two of them (newsletter, genre-community presence) are about deepening relationships you already have; one (asking beta readers for reviews) is about cashing in on relationships you've already built.

The marketing that works at book 1 is the marketing of relationships, not the marketing of reach.

## The won't-do list matters more than the do list

This is the lesson, stated explicitly: **the won't-do list is the more important of the two.**

For every tactic offered (paid course, paid promo site, expensive ad service, paid review service), the writer who has thought about the won't-do list has the answer ready. *Not for this book. Not at this scale of audience. Not at this stage of career.*

Without a won't-do list, every shiny tactic is a temptation. The launch becomes a vortex — three months on tactics that produce nothing, while book 2 sits unwritten.

The do list has three items. The temptation list has thirty. The won't-do list is what keeps the writer focused on the three.

The won't-do list is also self-protection during the slump. The week after launch, when sales drop, every paid tactic looks attractive. *Maybe one ad would help.* *Maybe a paid promo on that site I'd been refusing.* The won't-do list answers without the writer having to re-decide each time. It is pre-decision in service of the work.

The exercise at the end of this lesson is the saying-no.

## The book itself is the best marketing — for the *next* book

The single most reliable tactic for selling book 2 is having written a good book 1. Readers who finished and loved book 1 will check the back of it for the next title. Readers who skipped chapter four because it sagged will not.

The implication: most of what feels like marketing — the polish, the careful revision, the cuts in chapter four ([M5 L3-L4](/learn/lesson/process-revision-passes)) — *is* marketing. It has the longest tail of anything you can do.

First-time authors usually don't know this. They think marketing is the launch event. By book 3, the writers still in the game have figured out that book 3's marketing was the quality of book 1.

## The compound effect — book 3's marketing starts when book 1 ships

Imagine three books, each with two hundred readers at launch.

Book 1: the two hundred are the writer's pre-launch list. Some review. Some tell friends. By book 2, the list has grown to three or four hundred.

Book 2: launches to four hundred. Some convert from book 1's strangers. By book 3, the list is six or seven hundred.

Book 3: launches to seven hundred. The compound has begun.

The writer who treats book 1's launch as a marketing problem to solve in two weeks misses this. The writer who treats it as the *first move* in a three-book sequence builds.

## Try it

> **Exercise 6.4.1 — Two lists, three each.** Write two lists.
>
> **Will-do at launch (3 things).** Specific. Each item should fit the honest-tactics frame above and be something you can actually execute. *Send a five-email newsletter sequence over the four weeks before launch.* *Ask twelve specific beta readers for reviews.* *Be active in two genre forums in the year before launch.*
>
> **Won't-do at launch (3 things).** Specific tactics you are *refusing*. The list is shorter than the temptations; that's correct. Pick the three you'd be most likely to slip into. *No paid ads at this stage.* *No blog tour.* *No promo-site spending without retargeting data.*
>
> The won't-do list is the more useful of the two. Re-read it the week before launch, when the temptation is highest. Re-read it again the week after, when the panic of slow sales tries to renegotiate it.

## Further reading

- **For current platform launch features:** review the help documentation for the major retailer's launch tools, the platform's ad service if you choose to use it, and one current-year debut-author account of what worked at launch. The specifics shift annually.
- *An Autobiography* (Anthony Trollope, 1883). Trollope's discussion of how his books found readers in his lifetime is the cleanest pre-1900 description of *audience-as-asset* in English. The mechanics are nineteenth-century — circulating libraries, magazine serialisation — but the principle that a writer's previous book is the best marketing for the next is in the text.
