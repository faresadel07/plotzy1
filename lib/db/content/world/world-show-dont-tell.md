# Show, don't tell: the real meaning

*Show, don't tell* is the most quoted piece of writing advice in publishing.

It is also the most misapplied.

The advice usually arrives as if *telling* were a sin and *showing* were a virtue. That's wrong. Telling is what gets you across a year of fictional time in a paragraph. Telling is what makes a novel feel like a novel and not like a stage play.

The real rule is sharper.

:::quote
Show what matters; tell what doesn't.
:::

Both tools earn their place. The skill is knowing which moment wants which.

## What showing actually is

Showing renders a moment in real time. The reader is given the actions, the dialogue, the sensory detail; they reach the meaning by inference.

:::example side-by-side | The same moment, two ways
[Telling]
She was upset. Something had just happened that shook her badly.
[Showing]
She set the cup down. Her hand was shaking. Outside, snow had begun to fall.
:::

In the shown version, nobody says she is upset. The reader infers it, and the inference is the point.

Showing pulls the reader's simulator on: the [empathy machine from Module 1](/learn/lesson/foundation-why-people-read). Showing triggers the absorbed reader's low-resolution simulation most strongly; telling lets them observe rather than inhabit.

Showing is also *expensive*. A scene that could be told in two sentences might take two pages to show. That cost is the price of vividness: the reader's emotional investment is doubled when they reach the meaning themselves.

This is the case for showing. It is not the case against telling.

## What telling actually is

Telling compresses or summarises. It moves time, it bridges scenes, it gives the reader information without spending the page count of a rendered moment.

> The next twelve months passed quickly. By spring she was no longer flinching when his name came up.

Two sentences. A year of fictional time. Telling did in fifteen seconds what showing would have needed pages to do.

Telling is also how interiority happens at speed: a character's history, their frame of mind between two scenes, the texture of an off-stage relationship. Showing all of these every time would balloon the book.

Telling is what makes the novel a novel. The novelist's privilege over the playwright is summary: the ability to say *the next year passed quickly* and mean it. Writers ashamed of telling lose one of the form's primary tools.

## The real rule

Three questions to ask of any scene that is currently being *told*:

- Is this moment a turning point?
- Will the reader's emotional investment depend on having lived through this on the page?
- Is the reader currently being *told* something they should be *feeling*?

If yes to any: show it.

Three questions to ask of any scene that is currently being *shown*:

- Is this moment a turning point, or merely a transition?
- Could the reader catch up in two sentences?
- Is the showing rendering a moment, or is it a writer in love with their own prose?

If any answer is yes: tell it. Save the showing for the moments that matter.

These are the only six questions you need.

:::check
Q: Your protagonist spends three uneventful weeks travelling home between two major scenes. What does this stretch want?
- Showing, because travel scenes build immersion the reader will otherwise miss
-* Telling, because it is a transition the reader can cross in a sentence
- Showing, because skipping time breaks the reader's simulation
X: The journey is a transition, not a turning point. Summary carries the reader across it in seconds and saves the rendered pages for the moments that carry emotional weight.
:::

## The over-correction

There is a draft writers produce after they've internalised *show, don't tell* as a rule. Every scene is rendered. Every moment is shown. The book bulges from one hundred to two hundred thousand words.

It is exhausting to read.

The reader cannot feel the difference between a turning point and a transition because everything is rendered with equal weight. Emotional pacing flattens. The reader puts the book down around chapter eight and couldn't tell you why.

The cure is the rewrite of a scene from *show* to *tell*: the exercise at the end of this lesson. A draft that has been through this pass once has its rhythm back.

## Two writers, two choices

In *Pride and Prejudice*, the engagements are mostly *told*. The pivotal moment of Elizabeth and Darcy's reconciliation gets sentences, not scenes. *"It was settled between them at last that they should be married."* That sentence covers what other writers would render as a chapter.

Austen's choice is not laziness. It is *focus*. The engagements are not where her interest lies; the misjudgements that came before them are. She tells the engagement and shows the dance at Netherfield; the priority is built into the page count.

In *Wuthering Heights*, the moors are *shown*. The same novel that summarises whole years in a sentence will spend two pages on the wind across heather. The moors are where Brontë's interest lies; everything else is told around them.

Two great novels, two different choices about what to show, one shared rule.

## What this gives you

A test for every paragraph in your draft.

Permission to *tell*, which is what most beginners need before they can correctly *show*.

The next three lessons are downstream: sensory writing (L2) makes the shown moments land at line level, setting (L3) is when the *world itself* becomes a moment that wants showing, and description pacing (L4) is the discipline of cutting back to what earned its place.

## Try it

:::exercise
**Exercise 4.1.1: Two versions of the same paragraph.** Take a paragraph from your work in progress that *shows* a moment. Rewrite it as a paragraph that *tells* the same content. Be honest about what you lose; be honest about what you gain in pace.

Then ask: which version belongs in your novel? Sometimes the original. Sometimes the rewrite. Occasionally, usefully, neither, and the rewrite has shown you what the third version would be.

The point isn't to convert one into the other. The point is to learn the difference by feel, not by rule.
:::

:::cards
Showing | Rendering a moment in real time so the reader reaches the meaning by inference.
Telling | Compressing or summarising to move time, bridge scenes, and deliver information at speed.
The real rule | Show what matters; tell what doesn't.
Filter words | Verbs like saw, felt, and noticed that report a perception instead of letting the reader have it directly.
:::

:::takeaway
- Telling is not a sin: it moves time, bridges scenes, and keeps the novel a novel.
- Show turning points and moments the reader must live through; tell transitions and summary.
- Showing buys vividness at the cost of pages, so spend it where it matters.
- A draft where everything is shown flattens emotional pacing and exhausts the reader.
:::

## Further reading

All public domain. Free at Project Gutenberg.

- *Pride and Prejudice* (Austen, 1813). A masterclass in productive *telling*. Read the marriage chapters specifically: what Austen tells in a sentence, lesser writers render in a chapter. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/1342](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1342))
- *Wuthering Heights* (E. Brontë, 1847). For the contrast: extensive *showing* of the moors, set against tightly told family chronology. The same author moving between the two registers in the same book. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/768](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/768))
- *Madame Bovary* (Flaubert, 1856; the 1886 Eleanor Marx Aveling translation is PD). Studied for over a hundred and fifty years for when to render and when to summarise: the agricultural fair chapter is a textbook in alternating both within a single scene. ([gutenberg.org/ebooks/2413](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/2413))
